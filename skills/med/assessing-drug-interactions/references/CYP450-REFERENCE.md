# CYP450 Substrate, Inhibitor, and Inducer Reference

## Major CYP450 Enzymes

### CYP3A4 (metabolizes ~50% of drugs)

| Category | Common Drugs |
|----------|-------------|
| **Substrates** | Atorvastatin, simvastatin, amlodipine, cyclosporine, tacrolimus, midazolam, alprazolam, fentanyl, carbamazepine, apixaban, rivaroxaban |
| **Inhibitors** | Ketoconazole, itraconazole, clarithromycin, erythromycin, ritonavir, grapefruit juice, diltiazem, verapamil |
| **Inducers** | Rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, St. John's Wort, phenobarbital, efavirenz |

### CYP2D6 (metabolizes ~25% of drugs)

| Category | Common Drugs |
|----------|-------------|
| **Substrates** | Codeine (prodrug), tramadol, metoprolol, carvedilol, fluoxetine, paroxetine, venlafaxine, tamoxifen (prodrug), risperidone, aripiprazole |
| **Inhibitors** | Fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion, quinidine, duloxetine, terbinafine |
| **Inducers** | None clinically significant (not inducible) |

**Note**: CYP2D6 is polymorphic. Poor metabolizers (PM) accumulate substrates; ultra-rapid metabolizers (UM) may have reduced efficacy or toxicity from prodrugs.

### CYP2C19

| Category | Common Drugs |
|----------|-------------|
| **Substrates** | Clopidogrel (prodrug), omeprazole, esomeprazole, citalopram, escitalopram, diazepam, phenytoin, voriconazole |
| **Inhibitors** | Omeprazole, fluconazole, fluvoxamine, ticlopidine |
| **Inducers** | Rifampin, St. John's Wort |

**Note**: Polymorphic. PM + omeprazole = higher drug levels (good for acid suppression, but drug interaction risk). PM + clopidogrel = reduced activation (poor antiplatelet effect).

### CYP2C9

| Category | Common Drugs |
|----------|-------------|
| **Substrates** | Warfarin (S-enantiomer), phenytoin, losartan, celecoxib, glipizide, glimepiride |
| **Inhibitors** | Fluconazole, amiodarone, metronidazole, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole |
| **Inducers** | Rifampin, St. John's Wort |

### CYP1A2

| Category | Common Drugs |
|----------|-------------|
| **Substrates** | Theophylline, caffeine, clozapine, olanzapine, duloxetine, ramelteon, tizanidine |
| **Inhibitors** | Fluvoxamine, ciprofloxacin, enoxacin |
| **Inducers** | Smoking (tobacco), charcoal-broiled foods, rifampin, omeprazole |

**Note**: Smoking cessation can raise clozapine/olanzapine levels significantly — monitor within 1 week of quitting.

## High-Risk Interaction Patterns

| Pattern | Mechanism | Clinical Effect |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| Statin + strong CYP3A4 inhibitor | Elevated statin levels | Rhabdomyolysis risk |
| Warfarin + CYP2C9 inhibitor | Elevated warfarin | Bleeding risk — check INR |
| Codeine + CYP2D6 inhibitor | Blocked activation | Loss of analgesic effect |
| Clopidogrel + CYP2C19 inhibitor | Blocked activation | Loss of antiplatelet effect |
| Tamoxifen + CYP2D6 inhibitor | Blocked activation | Reduced breast cancer protection |

## QT-Prolonging Drug Combinations

Combining multiple QT-prolonging drugs increases risk of Torsades de Pointes.

**High-risk QT drugs**: amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, haloperidol, droperidol, methadone, ondansetron (IV high dose), fluoroquinolones, macrolides (azithromycin, erythromycin)

**Risk factors compounding QT prolongation**: hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, female sex, heart failure, hepatic impairment

## Serotonergic Drug Combinations

Combining serotonergic agents increases serotonin syndrome risk.

**Serotonergic drugs**: SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, tramadol, fentanyl, linezolid, methylene blue, triptans, lithium, St. John's Wort, dextromethorphan

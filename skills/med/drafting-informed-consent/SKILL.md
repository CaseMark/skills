# Drafting Informed Consent

Creates patient-facing consent documents balancing completeness with readability.

## Workflow

1. **Describe** procedure — what will happen, in plain language
2. **List** risks — common risks first, then rare-but-serious
3. **State** benefits — expected outcomes
4. **Present** alternatives — including no treatment
5. **Assess** readability — target 6th-8th grade reading level
6. **Include** required legal elements

## Output Template

```markdown
## Informed Consent: [Procedure Name]

### What Is This Procedure?
[Plain-language description — what happens, how long, anesthesia type]

### Why Is This Recommended?
[Clinical indication in patient terms]

### What Are the Risks?
**Common** (occur in more than 5% of patients):
- [Risk]

**Less Common** (occur in 1-5% of patients):
- [Risk]

**Rare but Serious** (occur in less than 1% of patients):
- [Risk]

### What Are the Benefits?
- [Expected benefit]

### What Are the Alternatives?
- [Alternative treatment options]
- [What happens without treatment]

### Questions to Ask Your Doctor
- [Suggested questions]

---
Patient Signature: _________________ Date: _______
Witness Signature: _________________ Date: _______
Provider Signature: _________________ Date: _______
```

## Key Rules

- Write at 6th-8th grade reading level (Flesch-Kincaid)
- Risk percentages should use consistent framing (not "1 in 100" mixed with "2%")
- Never minimize risks with reassuring language
- Include the option of declining treatment
- Separate common risks from rare-but-serious — patients process these differently
- Document that questions were invited and answered

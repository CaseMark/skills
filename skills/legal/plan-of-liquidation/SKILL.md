# Plan of Liquidation and Dissolution

Drafts a formal plan for winding down a U.S. corporation — satisfying creditors, distributing assets, and achieving legal dissolution in compliance with state statutes and federal tax rules.

## Prerequisites

1. **Corporate formation documents** — articles of incorporation, bylaws, any amendments (especially liquidation preferences for multiple share classes)
2. **Board resolution** — approving dissolution with vote count, quorum confirmation, date
3. **Shareholder approval** — meeting minutes or written consent showing requisite vote threshold met (majority or supermajority per jurisdiction/charter)
4. **Creditor inventory** — list of known creditors with addresses, amounts owed, secured/unsecured status
5. **Asset inventory** — real property, equipment, IP, receivables, investments, cash
6. **Pending obligations** — litigation, contracts, employee benefits, environmental liabilities
7. **State of incorporation** — jurisdiction-specific dissolution statute requirements

## Output Structure

### Section 1: Preamble & Recitals

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Corporate identity | Full legal name, state/date of incorporation, principal place of business |
| Statutory authority | Cite specific state dissolution statute (e.g., DGCL § 275, RMBCA § 14.02) [VERIFY] |
| Board determination | That dissolution serves best interests of corporation and shareholders |
| Business rationale | Brief factual basis — avoid detail that creates liability exposure |

### Section 2: Approval & Authorization

- Board vote: date, vote count, quorum confirmed
- Shareholder vote: date, shares voting for/against by class, statutory threshold met
- If written consent in lieu of meeting: document compliance with notice/consent requirements
- Separate class votes if preferred stock or other classes have special voting rights

### Section 3: Creditor & Stakeholder Notice

| Notice Type | Requirements |
|-------------|-------------|
| Known creditors | Certified mail or personal service; state claim deadline (typically 120+ days) |
| Unknown creditors | Publication in designated newspaper(s); frequency and duration per state statute |
| Employees | Termination notices, WARN Act compliance if applicable |
| Government | Tax authorities, regulatory agencies, license holders |
| Other | Customers, vendors, counterparties to material contracts |

Timeline must comply with state statute — notices typically sent within specified period after filing articles of dissolution.

### Section 4: Liquidation Process

**Priority waterfall for obligations:**

1. Costs and expenses of liquidation
2. Employee wages and benefits
3. Tax obligations
4. Secured creditor claims
5. Unsecured creditor claims
6. Shareholder distributions

**Operational wind-down checklist:**
- [ ] Complete or terminate existing contracts
- [ ] Collect accounts receivable
- [ ] Liquidate inventory and equipment
- [ ] Resolve pending litigation
- [ ] Address IP assets (assignment, abandonment, licensing)
- [ ] Establish reserves for contingent/disputed/unliquidated claims
- [ ] Identify liquidation oversight person or committee
- [ ] Engage professionals as needed (appraisers, auctioneers)

### Section 5: Asset Distribution

- Per-share calculation methodology for each share class
- Liquidation preferences and participation rights per articles
- Distribution timeline
- In-kind distributions: valuation method and allocation process
- **Tax treatment:** corporate gain/loss recognition; shareholder tax consequences (treated as exchange under IRC § 331) [VERIFY]
- Nonprofit entities: distribution to qualifying § 501(c)(3) organizations if applicable

### Section 6: Dissolution Filings

| Filing | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Articles/Certificate of Dissolution | File with Secretary of State; include corporate name, incorporation date, authorization statement, creditor notice confirmation, tax compliance statement |
| Tax clearance certificate | Required in many states before dissolution accepted |
| Final reports | State-specific annual/final report requirements |
| Regulatory consents | Industry-specific agency approvals if required |

Obtain and retain certified copies of all filed documents.

### Section 7: Tax & Compliance

- **IRS Form 966** — file within 30 days of adopting plan of dissolution
- **Final federal return** — Form 1120 (or variant), mark as final
- **Final state returns** — income, franchise, sales, employment tax
- Cancel EIN, sales tax permits, business licenses
- Obtain tax clearances from all relevant jurisdictions
- **Record retention** — maintain records 3–7 years post-dissolution (per statute and IRS requirements)

### Section 8: Effective Date & Execution

- Plan effective date and anticipated dissolution date
- Dissolution effective upon filing (or later date if specified in filing)
- Signature blocks: President and Secretary (or equivalent officers)
- Certification that signatories have authority and facts stated are accurate

## Guidelines

- **Jurisdiction-specific**: Dissolution procedures vary significantly by state — confirm all statutory citations, notice periods, and filing requirements against the state of incorporation
- **Fiduciary compliance**: Draft provisions demonstrating good faith, fair dealing, and compliance with duties to shareholders and creditors
- **Minority shareholder protection**: Anticipate potential challenges; ensure procedural fairness is documented
- **Special circumstances**: Flag and address pending litigation, environmental liabilities, ERISA plan terminations, or industry-specific regulatory issues
- **Formal drafting**: Use defined terms, consistent terminology, and precise language throughout
- **Do not** include speculative timelines — use bracketed placeholders for dates and amounts the client must confirm

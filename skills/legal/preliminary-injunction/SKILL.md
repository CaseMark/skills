# Motion for Preliminary Injunction

Drafts a procedurally compliant, persuasive motion for preliminary injunction that satisfies the applicable multi-factor test and builds a record for appellate review.

## Prerequisites

1. **Complaint or petition** — filed or ready to file; causes of action identified
2. **Case documents** — contracts, correspondence, declarations, exhibits supporting the factual narrative
3. **Court and jurisdiction** — specific court, case number, and applicable rules (FRCP 65 or state equivalent)
4. **Evidence of harm** — financial data, expert analyses, communications, or other proof of imminent irreparable injury
5. **Proposed injunctive terms** — specific conduct to be enjoined or compelled

## Output Structure

### 1. Jurisdictional & Procedural Research

Before drafting, determine:

| Item | Action |
|---|---|
| Governing rule | FRCP 65 or state equivalent; identify local rule variants |
| Injunction standard | Winter four-factor (strict) vs. sliding scale; circuit/state-specific gloss |
| Presumptions | IP cases, constitutional claims, or other subject-matter presumptions |
| Local rules | Caption format, page limits, font/margins, briefing schedule, proposed order requirements |
| Bond requirement | FRCP 65(c) or state equivalent; factors for setting amount |
| Emergency procedures | TRO availability, ex parte requirements, expedited hearing process |

### 2. Caption & Procedural Framework

- Full party names matching the complaint
- Court name including division/department
- Case number and any required designations
- Title: "Plaintiff's Motion for Preliminary Injunction"
- Notice of hearing date or request for expedited consideration
- Identify simultaneous filings (proposed order, declarations, exhibits)

### 3. Introduction (1-2 pages)

- State exactly what conduct the court should enjoin/compel — in order-ready language
- Identify the procedural authority (Rule 65 or equivalent)
- Preview why plaintiff satisfies each factor
- Frame consequences of inaction in concrete terms

### 4. Statement of Facts

- Chronological narrative drawn from case documents
- Cite specific exhibits, dates, amounts, and verbatim quotes
- Distinguish undisputed facts from contested facts (note evidentiary support for contested facts)
- No legal conclusions — let facts drive the reader to your argument

### 5. Legal Standard

State the applicable test with controlling authority:

```
The Winter four-factor test requires plaintiff to demonstrate:
1. Likelihood of success on the merits
2. Likelihood of irreparable harm absent injunction
3. Balance of equities tips in plaintiff's favor
4. Public interest favors injunction

Winter v. NRDC, 555 U.S. 7 (2008)
```

Note any circuit-specific variations (sliding scale, "serious questions" alternative, heightened standards for mandatory injunctions).

### 6. Factor Analysis

#### A. Likelihood of Success on the Merits

For each cause of action:

| Step | Content |
|---|---|
| Elements | State elements with controlling citation |
| Application | Map facts to each element with record cites |
| Defenses | Preemptively address likely defenses and distinguish |
| Adverse authority | Distinguish unfavorable cases (factual differences, superseded, non-controlling) |
| Threshold issues | Standing, ripeness, limitations, preemption — explain why they don't bar relief |

#### B. Irreparable Harm

- Identify specific, imminent, non-speculative harm
- Explain why monetary damages are inadequate (loss of goodwill, trade secret disclosure, constitutional deprivation, market destruction, unique property)
- Cite jurisdiction-specific cases recognizing this category of harm as irreparable
- Quantify where possible but emphasize qualitative irreparability
- Show imminence — what defendant is doing or will do, and when

#### C. Balance of Equities

- Acknowledge defendant's burdens candidly
- Argue plaintiff's harm outweighs because:
  - Defendant's hardship is self-inflicted (caused by wrongful conduct)
  - Defendant's harm is economic and bond-compensable; plaintiff's is not
  - Injunction preserves status quo rather than mandating affirmative action
- If mandatory injunction is sought, justify the heightened burden

#### D. Public Interest

- Connect to enforcement of applicable law, constitutional values, or regulatory policy
- Address competing public interests and explain why plaintiff's interests predominate
- For government defendants: emphasize public interest in constitutional compliance

### 7. Conclusion & Prayer for Relief

- Synthesize the four factors in 1-2 paragraphs
- Draft specific injunctive terms in order-ready language:

```
WHEREFORE, Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court:

1. [Specific prohibitory relief — exact conduct to be enjoined]
2. [Specific mandatory relief, if any — exact conduct to be compelled]
3. [Duration or conditions]
4. [Bond amount proposal or request for waiver with justification]
5. [Request for expedited hearing / TRO if applicable]
6. [Alternative narrower relief formulations]
```

### 8. Signature Block & Service

- Attorney name, bar number, firm, address, phone, email
- Dated signature line per local format rules
- Certificate of service with method and date
- Verification language if required by jurisdiction

## Guidelines

- **Specificity in injunctive terms**: Every prohibited/required act must be precise enough for enforcement and contempt proceedings
- **Record citations**: Every factual assertion must cite a declaration, exhibit, or document
- **Bluebook citations**: All legal citations in proper Bluebook format
- **Status quo bias**: Frame relief as preserving the status quo wherever possible; courts disfavor mandatory injunctions
- **Bond**: Always address bond — propose an amount with rationale, or argue for nominal/no bond with supporting authority
- **TRO bridge**: If harm is truly imminent, include or separately file a TRO request explaining why notice is impracticable (if ex parte) or requesting shortened response time
- **Jurisdiction-specific research is mandatory**: The standard varies materially across circuits and states — never assume Winter alone controls
- **Page limits**: Check local rules; many courts impose 25-page limits on motions — allocate space accordingly
- **Supporting declarations**: The motion should reference (not replace) declarations from witnesses with personal knowledge

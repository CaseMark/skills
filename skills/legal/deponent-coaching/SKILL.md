# Deponent Coaching Guide

Produces ready-to-use behavioral coaching materials for a witness preparing for deposition, covering methodology, rules, difficult question handling, and composure techniques.

## Prerequisites

1. **Witness identity and role** — who the deponent is relative to the matter
2. **Matter context** — general subject matter and claims/defenses at issue
3. **Anticipated difficulty areas** — known topics where the witness may struggle (optional but improves output)

## Output Structure

### 1. Core Mindset

Open every coaching session by establishing:

> "Your only job is to tell the truth based on what you actually know and remember. You are not there to win the case or help either side — only to answer truthfully. If the truth hurts our case, tell it anyway. What we cannot recover from is testimony that isn't true."

---

### 2. The SHAQ Method

| Step | Rule | Key Point |
|------|------|-----------|
| **S**top | Pause before answering | Don't formulate while the question is still being asked |
| **H**ear | Listen to the full question | Wait for completion; ask for clarification if unclear |
| **A**nswer | Answer only what was asked | Yes/no questions get yes/no answers — no volunteering |
| **Q**uit | Stop talking immediately | Silence after answering is not your problem |

**SHAQ Example:**
- Q: "Did you attend the March 15 meeting?"
- ❌ "Yes, I attended. It was about the quarterly review. About eight people were there..."
- ✅ "Yes."

---

### 3. The Ten Golden Rules

| # | Rule | Script / Application |
|---|------|----------------------|
| 1 | Listen to the complete question | Never interrupt; don't anticipate the ending |
| 2 | Clarify before answering | "What do you mean by [term]?" / "Can you rephrase that?" |
| 3 | Answer only what's asked | No explanations unless asked "why" |
| 4 | Always tell the truth | Even unfavorable facts — partial lies destroy all credibility |
| 5 | "I don't know" is acceptable | Use when you genuinely lack the information |
| 6 | "I don't recall" is acceptable | Use when you may have known but cannot now remember |
| 7 | Take your time | No prize for speed — pause, think, then answer |
| 8 | Don't guess or speculate | "I don't recall the date" not "I think it was probably March" |
| 9 | Reject false characterizations | "I wouldn't characterize it that way." / "That's not accurate." |
| 10 | Don't look to your attorney | They can object; they cannot answer for you |

---

### 4. Difficult Question Types

| Question Type | Example | How to Handle |
|---------------|---------|---------------|
| **Compound** | "Did you review and approve it?" | "That's two questions. I reviewed it. I did not approve it." |
| **False premise** | "When did you stop falsifying reports?" | "I never falsified any reports." — do not answer the "when" |
| **"Isn't it true that..."** | "Isn't it true you were angry?" | Agree if true; correct if false; qualify if partial: "I was frustrated, not angry." |
| **Absolutes ("always/never")** | "You always followed the policy, correct?" | "I followed it as a general practice" — avoid absolute commitments you can't guarantee |
| **Hypotheticals** | "What would you have done if..." | "I can only speak to what I actually did, not what I might have done." |
| **Unrecognized documents** | "Do you recognize this document?" | "I don't recognize this document." / "I don't recall seeing this before." |
| **Opinion/characterization** | "Do you think the company acted fairly?" | "I can tell you what I observed." — stay in facts, not judgments |

---

### 5. Composure Under Pressure

**When opposing counsel is aggressive:**
- Slow down — don't speed up
- Take a sip of water (natural pause, resets composure)
- Don't match their tone or energy
- Aggressiveness is a tactic, not personal — your job is still just to answer truthfully

**If you realize you made a mistake mid-deposition:**
> "I need to correct something I said earlier..."

Correct it immediately. Don't hope no one noticed. Corrections reflect honesty; cover-ups reflect deception.

**Break rules:**
- ✅ May request a break for restroom or to collect yourself
- ❌ Cannot take a break while a question is pending — must answer first
- ❌ Cannot consult attorney about how to answer a specific question during a break

### 6. Quick-Reference Phrase Card

*(Provide this page directly to the deponent)*

| Situation | What to Say |
|-----------|-------------|
| Don't understand the question | "I don't understand the question." / "Can you rephrase that?" |
| Don't know the answer | "I don't know." / "I don't have that information." |
| Don't remember | "I don't recall." / "I don't have a specific recollection of that." |
| Question assumes something false | "That's not accurate." + state what is accurate |
| Need a moment | Take a breath — there is no rush to answer |

**Never say:**
- "Honestly..." or "To tell you the truth..." — implies prior answers were not honest
- "I think" when you actually know — reserve "I think" for genuine uncertainty

**Never do:**
- Nod or shake your head — the court reporter requires verbal answers
- Volunteer information beyond the scope of the question
- Argue, be sarcastic, or make jokes — these read terribly in a transcript

## Guidelines

- **Ethics boundary**: Coaching must address *how* to testify — not *what* to say. Do not suggest substance, shape recollection, or help a witness avoid truthful but harmful facts. See ABA Formal Opinion 508 (2023) [VERIFY].
- **Speculation vs. estimate**: If the questioner explicitly asks "Do you have any idea?" or "Can you estimate?", the witness may offer a qualified answer — this differs from unprompted guessing.
- **Jurisdiction**: Behavioral coaching rules apply uniformly across U.S. federal and state courts; no jurisdiction-specific variations required.
- **Companion skills**: Use with `@deposition-witness-prep-session` for the full session framework; `@deposition-objection-reference` for explaining what happens when counsel objects during testimony.
- **References**: ABA Formal Opinion 508 (2023) [VERIFY]; NITA Deposition Skills Training Materials; Malone & Hoffman, *The Effective Deposition* (4th ed.).

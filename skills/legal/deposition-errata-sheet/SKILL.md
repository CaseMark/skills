# Deposition Errata Sheet

Manages the FRCP 30(e) errata process for your witness or against an opposing witness's improper corrections.

## Prerequisites

1. **Deposition transcript** — full transcript with page/line numbers
2. **Review request status** — confirm review was requested before deposition concluded
3. **Deadline** — 30 days from reporter's notification that transcript is available
4. **Audio/video recording** (if any) — for verifying transcription errors
5. **Jurisdiction** — local circuit/district precedent on scope of permissible changes

## Output Structure / Process

### Rule Framework: FRCP 30(e)

Deponent must be allowed 30 days after notification to review and sign a statement listing changes "in form or substance" with reasons. Request must be made **before the deposition concludes**.

### Jurisdictional Split on Substantive Changes

| View | Rule | Key Cases |
|------|------|-----------|
| **Permissive** | Both form and substance allowed; original preserved; inconsistencies addressed on cross | *Greenway v. Int'l Paper Co.*, 144 F.R.D. 322 (W.D. La. 1992) |
| **Restrictive** | Only transcription errors; substantive changes may be stricken | Various district courts |
| **Intermediate (majority)** | Substantive changes technically permitted but contradictory changes disregarded; reasons scrutinized; usable for impeachment | *Hambleton Bros. Lumber Co. v. Balkin Enters.*, 397 F.3d 1217 (9th Cir. 2005); *Burns v. Bd. of Cty. Comm'rs*, 330 F.3d 1275 (10th Cir. 2003) |

**Always verify controlling precedent in your jurisdiction before submitting or challenging errata.**

### Part A — Submitting Errata (Your Witness)

**Change Permissibility**

| Category | Examples | Permissible? |
|----------|----------|--------------|
| Typographical error | Name misspelled, number wrong | ✅ Yes |
| Technical/industry term | Acronym expanded wrong, jargon misheard | ✅ Yes |
| Obvious mishearing | "I didn't" vs. "I did"; audio confirms | ✅ Yes |
| Witness cut off | Answer incomplete in transcript | ✅ Yes |
| Substantive clarification | "What I meant was..." | ⚠️ Scrutinized |
| Memory refreshed post-depo | Reviewed docs, recalls differently | ⚠️ Scrutinized |
| Complete reversal | "Yes" → "No" | ❌ Improper |
| Adding new facts | Facts not discussed at deposition | ❌ Improper |
| Damage control | Changing harmful admissions | ❌ Improper |

**Strategic Filter Before Correcting**
- Does the error significantly misrepresent testimony or create a false admission?
- Can you articulate a legitimate, non-pretextual reason?
- Will the correction draw more attention to unfavorable testimony than leaving it alone?
- Will opposing counsel convert the change into an impeachment opportunity?

**Errata Sheet Template**

```
                        ERRATA SHEET

Case:              [CASE NAME AND NUMBER]
Deponent:          [NAME]
Deposition Date:   [DATE]
Review Date:       [DATE]

I have reviewed the transcript of my deposition and request the
following corrections:

Page  Line  Reads                 Should Read           Reason
----  ----  --------------------  --------------------  --------------------
                                                        
                                                        
                                                        

Subject to the corrections noted above, I affirm the transcript
is a true and accurate record of my testimony.

I declare under penalty of perjury that the foregoing is true
and correct.

Executed on _____________, at ________________.

                         _________________________________
                         [DEPONENT SIGNATURE / PRINTED NAME]
```

**Submission Checklist**
- [ ] Review requested on the record before deposition closed
- [ ] 30-day deadline verified
- [ ] Full transcript reviewed with witness
- [ ] Compared to audio/video recording if available
- [ ] Each change evaluated for propriety and credibility cost
- [ ] Clear, non-pretextual reason stated for each change
- [ ] Witness signed under oath
- [ ] Served on all parties and provided to court reporter

### Part B — Challenging Opposing Errata

**Red Flags for Improper Corrections**
- Complete reversal of key testimony (Yes → No)
- Facts added that were never discussed at deposition
- Every change benefits the changing party
- Vague or conclusory reasons ("error in transcript")
- Changes timed to pending motion deadlines

**Response Options**

| Option | When to Use | Approach |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Motion to Strike** | Restrictive jurisdiction; changes are substantive | Argue changes exceed FRCP 30(e) scope; request court rely on original transcript |
| **Opposition to Reliance** | Opposing party cites changed testimony in briefing | Attach original transcript excerpt; argue changes are pretextual and should be disregarded |
| **Impeachment at Trial** | Preserve credibility attack | Use original answer, then expose the change and its timing |

**Impeachment Framework**
```
Q: At your deposition you testified [ORIGINAL ANSWER], correct?
Q: You gave that answer under oath?
Q: After the deposition you submitted an errata sheet changing that answer?
Q: You changed [N] answers total in your errata sheet?
Q: Every single change benefited your case, didn't it?
```

**Locking in Testimony During Deposition (Preemptive)**
- "Is that your testimony today?"
- "You've had time to think about this question?"
- "You understand you can correct transcription errors in the errata sheet, but not change the substance of your answers?"

**Receive-Opposing-Errata Checklist**
- [ ] Identified all substantive vs. typographical changes
- [ ] Researched controlling local precedent on permissible scope
- [ ] Selected response strategy (strike / oppose reliance / impeach)
- [ ] Preserved original transcript for trial impeachment
- [ ] Considered whether supplemental deposition is warranted (if new facts added)

## Guidelines

- Courts vary sharply on substantive changes — the intermediate/majority view tolerates them but uses scrutiny; never assume permissive treatment in unfamiliar districts
- Reasons for each change are legally required and strategically critical — vague reasons invite adverse inference
- Never use errata to reverse testimony solely because it became strategically inconvenient; ethics rules on candor apply
- State court rules differ from FRCP 30(e); verify state analog before applying this framework in state court proceedings
- Original testimony is never destroyed — the changed version sits alongside it, making every improper change a built-in impeachment document

# Demand Letter — Example Corpus Manifest

All examples below are publicly-available documents. Court exhibits are part of
the public record under the common-law right of access to judicial records. US
government documents (CFPB, HUD, court-published forms, etc.) are public domain
under 17 U.S.C. § 105. Party and attorney names are preserved as they appear in
the original filings — we do not redact public-record names.

Every file in this corpus is a **real demand letter** — a pre-suit (or
pre-enforcement) communication from counsel asserting a claim, making specific
demands with a deadline, and stating consequences for non-compliance. Responses
to demand letters, termination letters, motion-to-dismiss exhibits that merely
reference demand letters, and other adjacent artifacts are not included here.

## Known coverage gaps

clean exhibit attachments were located. Better sourcing for these is via
law-firm or state-bar/AG published sample-letter banks.

| Archetype | Why desired |
|---|---|

| FDCPA § 1692g / collection demand letter | High-volume consumer category |

| Construction defect notice (CA SB 800, TX Ch. 27, FL Ch. 558) | High-dollar statutory prerequisite |
| Commercial breach + cure notice | Largest practice area by volume |

| FLSA pre-suit unpaid-wages demand | Employees typically skip to complaint |
| TX Stowers demand | Multiple searches surfaced only motion-to-dismiss refs |

## Collection method

All court-exhibit PDFs sourced via `casedev search cases "<query>"` against the
CourtListener / RECAP archive. Every candidate was verified via
`pdftotext -layout` inspection before inclusion — the document itself must be a
demand letter, not a pleading that references one.

## OCR companion

`defamation-retraction-minc.pdf` is image-only. It was run through case.dev
vision (doctr) via a temporary vault and produced:

- `defamation-retraction-minc.ocr.json` — consolidated output: provenance,
  clean-text chunks, per-page words with bboxes and per-word confidence
- `defamation-retraction-minc.ocr.md` — readable markdown with reading-order
  reconstructed from bbox sort

Mean OCR confidence 93.3%. Raw OCR artifacts ("FMAND" for "DEMAND", etc.) are
preserved verbatim; the markdown note calls them out so a reader does not
attribute them to the source document.

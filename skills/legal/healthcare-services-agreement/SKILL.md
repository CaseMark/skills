# Professional Services Agreement — Healthcare

Drafts a healthcare-specific professional services agreement that balances clinical autonomy with regulatory compliance and appropriate risk allocation.

## Prerequisites

1. **Party information** — full legal names, entity types, tax IDs, and signing authority for both sides
2. **Provider credentials** — license numbers, NPI, DEA registration (if applicable), board certifications, hospital privileges
3. **Service scope** — specialties, procedures, locations (in-person / telehealth), volume expectations, on-call requirements
4. **Compensation terms** — rate structure, billing responsibility (provider-direct vs. client-billed), expense policy
5. **Insurance details** — current malpractice policy type (claims-made vs. occurrence), coverage limits, CGL policy
6. **HIPAA role designation** — whether provider is Covered Entity, Business Associate, or workforce member relative to client
7. **Governing jurisdiction** — state(s) where services will be rendered

## Output Structure

### Article 1 — Parties & Recitals

| Field | Provider | Client |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Legal name & entity type | ✓ | ✓ |
| Principal address | ✓ | ✓ |
| Tax ID / EIN | ✓ | ✓ |
| Credentials (license #, NPI, DEA) | ✓ | — |
| Capacity (individual / PC / LLC / group) | ✓ | — |
| Signing authority & title | ✓ | ✓ |

State explicitly: **independent contractor relationship** — not employment, partnership, or joint venture.

### Article 2 — Scope of Services

- Enumerate services with clinical specificity; avoid vague generalities
- Reference applicable **standard of care** for the relevant specialty and jurisdiction
- State locations and modalities (on-site, telehealth, client facility)
- Define scheduling, on-call obligations, and response-time expectations
- List **exclusions** — services NOT covered
- Address emergency protocols and out-of-scope situations
- If experimental/non-standard procedures are contemplated, require separate informed consent provisions
- Include credentialing/privileging requirements for facility-based services

### Article 3 — Compensation & Billing

- Specify rate structure: fee-for-service / hourly / per-patient / retainer / hybrid
- Include rate schedule as exhibit if complex
- Define billing cycle, invoice format, payment terms (net 30/60), accepted methods
- Assign billing responsibility: who bills patients and/or third-party payers
- Allocate risk of uncollectible accounts
- Define reimbursable expenses, pre-approval process, and documentation requirements
- Rate adjustment mechanism: notice period, cap, CPI index if applicable

**Regulatory guardrail:** Compensation must be at fair market value and commercially reasonable. Structure must not vary with volume or value of referrals. Include affirmative representation of compliance with:
- Anti-Kickback Statute (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b) [VERIFY]
- Stark Law physician self-referral prohibition (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn) [VERIFY]

### Article 4 — Term & Termination

| Provision | Standard |
|-----------|----------|
| Initial term | Fixed dates or rolling term |
| Auto-renewal | Yes/No; opt-out notice 60–90 days before expiration |
| Termination without cause | 90–180 days' written notice |
| Termination for cause (with cure) | 30-day notice and cure period |
| Immediate termination triggers | See list below |

**Immediate termination triggers** (no cure period):
- [ ] Loss or suspension of medical license
- [ ] DEA registration revocation
- [ ] Exclusion from Medicare/Medicaid/federal healthcare programs
- [ ] Felony conviction or indictment
- [ ] Professional misconduct finding
- [ ] Failure to maintain required insurance
- [ ] Material HIPAA breach

**Transition obligations on termination:**
- Continued service during notice period
- Patient care continuity plan and warm handoff
- Medical records transfer per state retention laws
- Return of client property, credentials, access
- Pro-rata compensation through termination date

### Article 5 — HIPAA & Confidentiality

1. **Designate HIPAA roles** — Covered Entity vs. Business Associate vs. workforce member
2. If BA relationship exists: attach **Business Associate Agreement** as exhibit per 45 CFR § 164.504(e)
3. BAA must address:
   - Permitted uses/disclosures of PHI
   - Administrative, physical, and technical safeguards
   - Breach notification timeline (without unreasonable delay, no later than 60 days per HITECH)
   - Subcontractor flow-down requirements
   - Audit and compliance monitoring rights
   - Return/destruction of PHI on termination
4. **Non-HIPAA confidentiality** — proprietary business information, trade secrets
   - Survival: indefinite for PHI; 3–5 years for business confidential information
   - Secure storage, transmission, and disposal requirements

### Article 6 — Representations & Warranties

**Provider represents and warrants:**
- [ ] Current, unrestricted license(s) in applicable jurisdiction(s)
- [ ] DEA registration (if prescribing controlled substances)
- [ ] Board certification in relevant specialty (if claimed)
- [ ] Not excluded from federal healthcare programs (check OIG exclusion list)
- [ ] No pending disciplinary actions, malpractice claims, or investigations that impair ability to perform
- [ ] Disclosure of any prior license suspension, restriction, or discipline
- [ ] Compliance with state medical practice acts and professional ethics standards
- [ ] Immediate notification obligation if any warranty becomes untrue

**Client represents and warrants:**
- [ ] Authority to contract; no conflicting obligations
- [ ] Maintenance of required facility licenses, accreditations, and permits
- [ ] Compliance with applicable healthcare regulations

### Article 7 — Indemnification

Mutual indemnification structure:

| Indemnifying Party | Covers |
|--------------------|--------|
| Provider | Professional negligence/malpractice, failure to meet standard of care, regulatory violations, confidentiality breach |
| Client | Client negligence, defective equipment/facilities, failure to maintain licenses/accreditations, breach of client obligations |

- Scope: damages, settlements, judgments, reasonable attorneys' fees
- Exclude indemnification for indemnitor's gross negligence or willful misconduct
- Procedure: prompt written notice → cooperation in defense → mutual consent for settlements
- Optional: liability cap (except for indemnification arising from willful misconduct, gross negligence, or HIPAA violations)

### Article 8 — Insurance

| Coverage | Minimum Limits | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Professional liability (malpractice) | $1M per occurrence / $3M aggregate (adjust for high-risk specialties) | Specify claims-made vs. occurrence; if claims-made, require tail coverage on termination |
| Commercial general liability | $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate | |
| Cyber liability | Per risk profile | Required if provider handles ePHI |
| Workers' compensation | Statutory | If provider has employees |

- Client named as additional insured on applicable policies
- Certificates of insurance due before services commence and at each renewal
- 30 days' advance written notice of cancellation, non-renewal, or material change
- Insurance minimums do not cap indemnification obligations

### Article 9 — Dispute Resolution

**Tiered process:**
1. Good-faith negotiation between senior executives — 30 days
2. Mediation with healthcare-experienced mediator — costs split equally
3. Binding arbitration under AAA Healthcare Payor Provider Arbitration Rules (or litigation, per party preference)

- Carve-out: either party may seek emergency injunctive relief in court for confidentiality breaches or irreparable harm
- Governing law: state where services are primarily rendered
- Exclusive venue: courts of the governing-law state

### Article 10 — General Provisions

- **Severability** with replacement negotiation obligation
- **Amendments** in writing only, signed by authorized representatives
- **Notices** — specify addresses, methods (personal delivery, certified mail, overnight courier, email), deemed-receipt rules
- **Assignment** prohibited without written consent; carve-out for successors/affiliates
- **Entire agreement** — supersedes all prior negotiations
- **Waiver** — no implied waiver from failure to enforce
- **Force majeure** — standard clause, but patient care obligations are NOT excused
- **Survival** — confidentiality, indemnification, payment obligations, and insurance tail requirements survive termination
- **Counterparts and electronic signatures** permitted

### Signature Block

Include for each party: signature line, printed name, title, date. For entities, ensure signatory has binding authority. Include representation of authority.

## Guidelines

1. **Anti-Kickback / Stark compliance** is non-negotiable — compensation must be FMV, fixed in advance, and not tied to referral volume
2. Always confirm provider's OIG exclusion status before finalizing
3. BAA is mandatory if provider qualifies as a Business Associate — omission creates HIPAA liability for both parties
4. Tail coverage requirement is critical for claims-made policies; omission creates a coverage gap
5. State medical practice acts vary significantly — verify scope-of-practice and corporate-practice-of-medicine restrictions for the applicable state
6. If telehealth services are included, address multi-state licensure requirements and state-specific telehealth regulations
7. Patient care continuity provisions on termination are an ethical obligation, not merely contractual — ensure compliance with AMA ethics guidance and state abandonment rules
8. Do not include non-compete clauses without confirming enforceability under applicable state law (many states restrict or ban physician non-competes)

# Insurance Coverage Opinion

Analyzes policy language, complaint allegations, and state law to produce a definitive coverage opinion guiding carrier decisions on defense, indemnification, and reservation of rights.

## Prerequisites

1. **Insurance policy** — declarations page, coverage forms, all endorsements, applicable policy period
2. **Complaint or claim documents** — the operative pleading or demand triggering coverage analysis
3. **Supporting materials** — police reports, claim file notes, correspondence (if available)
4. **Controlling jurisdiction** — state whose insurance law governs interpretation

## Output Structure

### 1. Executive Summary

Single paragraph, written last, placed first:

> "Based on our analysis of the [Policy Type] policy and the allegations in the complaint, [Carrier] has [no duty to defend / a duty to defend subject to a reservation of rights / a clear duty to defend and indemnify] because [primary reason]."

- Be definitive — avoid "probably" or "might"
- State the bottom-line recommendation upfront

### 2. Factual Background

- Recite only facts alleged in the complaint or established in the claim file
- No speculation or interpretation beyond the record
- Focus on coverage-relevant facts only
- Maintain neutral tone

### 3. Policy Analysis

Structure as three sequential sub-sections:

| Sub-Section | Requirements |
|---|---|
| **Coverage Provisions** | Quote exact policy language. Identify specific coverage section (CGL Coverage A, Professional Liability, etc.) |
| **Exclusions Analysis** | List every potentially applicable exclusion with exact quoted language. This is typically where coverage is defeated. |
| **Conditions & Other Provisions** | Notice requirements, cooperation clauses, late-notice defenses, consent-to-settle provisions |

**Critical**: Always quote exact policy language — never paraphrase.

### 4. Legal Analysis

#### Duty to Defend

Apply the **Eight Corners Rule** (unless state law permits extrinsic evidence):

- [ ] Compare four corners of complaint against four corners of policy
- [ ] Assess element-by-element whether allegations could trigger coverage
- [ ] Analyze each exclusion for clear and unambiguous applicability
- [ ] Apply the "any possibility of coverage" standard — duty to defend is broad

States departing from strict eight corners (allow extrinsic evidence): varies by jurisdiction — research and cite controlling authority.

#### Duty to Indemnify

- Narrower standard — based on actual facts, not allegations
- Usually cannot be determined until case resolution
- Default conclusion: "The duty to indemnify cannot be determined at this time"

### 5. State Law Considerations

Research and cite controlling jurisdiction on:

| Issue | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Eight corners vs. extrinsic evidence | Determines what the court may consider |
| Ambiguity interpretation | Most states construe ambiguity pro-insured |
| Specific coverage constructions | State-specific rules on exclusion types |
| Recent case law | Insurance coverage law evolves rapidly — cite current authority |

Mark any uncertain citations with [VERIFY].

### 6. Conclusion & Recommendations

Use definitive language matching one of three outcomes:

- **No coverage**: "[Carrier] has no duty to defend or indemnify because [exclusion] unambiguously bars coverage for the alleged conduct."
- **Questionable coverage**: "[Carrier] should defend under a reservation of rights because [reason], while investigating [specific issues]."
- **Clear coverage**: "[Carrier] has a duty to defend and likely a duty to indemnify based on the allegations."

Include practical recommendations:

- [ ] Whether to issue a reservation of rights letter (and draft key language if so)
- [ ] Specific rights being reserved
- [ ] Additional investigation needed
- [ ] Coverage defenses to preserve
- [ ] Timeline considerations

### 7. Reservation of Rights Discussion

When coverage is questionable, default to recommending reservation. Specify:
- Which coverage defenses are preserved
- That defending under reservation protects later denial rights
- Suggested language for the reservation letter

## Guidelines

- **Perspective**: You represent the carrier, not the insured — but analysis must be objective; wrongful denial exposes the carrier to bad faith liability
- **Definitiveness**: Carriers need actionable guidance, not hedging; when genuinely uncertain, recommend defend-under-reservation
- **No added facts**: Never assume facts not alleged in the complaint
- **Duty to defend ≠ duty to indemnify**: Always analyze separately with distinct standards
- **Turnaround**: Flag timing — coverage decisions affect litigation strategy; communicate delays immediately
- **Updateability**: Note that the opinion may require revision as facts develop
- **Citation currency**: Insurance law evolves rapidly; verify all cited authority is current

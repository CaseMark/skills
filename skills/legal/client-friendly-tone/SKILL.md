# Client-Friendly Communication Tone

Apply plain-language, client-centered writing style to all client-facing legal documents.

## Prerequisites

1. Draft or source text to rewrite
2. Audience context — sophistication level, prior communications, emotional state of matter

## Applicable Document Types

- Engagement letters
- Status updates
- Strategy memos
- Settlement recommendations
- Invoice cover letters
- Any client-facing correspondence

## Core Rules

| Rule | Implementation |
|------|---------------|
| Lead with bottom line | First sentence states the conclusion, recommendation, or required action |
| Plain English | Replace legal terms with everyday language on first use; parenthetical original term if needed for record |
| Define then abbreviate | On first use: plain explanation + legal term in parentheses. Subsequent uses: legal term only |
| Highlight action items | Bold or bullet all items requiring client decision or response, with deadlines |
| Explain costs and risks | State financial exposure and likelihood in concrete terms, not abstractions |
| Acknowledge concerns | Open with brief empathetic framing when matter is stressful or outcome is adverse |
| Structure for scanning | Use headers, bullets, and short paragraphs — no walls of text |
| Anticipate questions | Preemptively address the 2-3 most likely client follow-ups |

## Jargon Replacement Reference

| Legal Term | Client-Friendly Version |
|------------|------------------------|
| Motion for summary judgment | A request asking the judge to rule in our favor without a trial |
| Discovery | The formal process of exchanging information with the other side |
| Statute of limitations | The deadline for filing this type of lawsuit |
| Deposition | A formal interview under oath, recorded by a court reporter |
| Interrogatories | Written questions the other side must answer under oath |
| Stipulation | An agreement between both sides on a specific point |
| Continuance | A postponement of a court date |
| Prejudice / without prejudice | Permanently / with the option to refile |

Extend this pattern to any legal term not commonly understood by a non-lawyer audience.

## Document Structure Template

```
RE: [Matter name — plain description]

[Bottom line: 1-2 sentences with conclusion or recommendation]

Background
[Brief context — only what client needs to understand the update]

What This Means for You
[Implications in concrete, practical terms — costs, timeline, risks]

Next Steps
- [ ] [Action item + who is responsible + deadline]
- [ ] [Action item + who is responsible + deadline]

Questions?
[Preemptive answers to likely follow-ups]
```

## Guidelines

- Never assume client remembers prior legal terminology — redefine if more than 30 days since last use
- Avoid hedging chains ("it is possible that it may potentially...") — state likelihood directly
- Use concrete numbers and dates over vague ranges when available
- Do not omit legal nuance — simplify the language, not the substance
- Match formality to relationship stage: engagement letters are more formal; ongoing updates can be conversational

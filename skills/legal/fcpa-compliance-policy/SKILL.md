# FCPA Compliance Policy

Drafts a litigation-ready, immediately implementable FCPA Compliance Policy anchored in DOJ/SEC enforcement guidance, with specific thresholds, approval workflows, and role-based obligations.

## Prerequisites

1. **Company profile** — jurisdiction of incorporation, whether SEC issuer or domestic concern, geographic markets and high-risk countries
2. **Existing compliance materials** — prior FCPA policies, internal audit findings, prior enforcement history, third-party agent inventory
3. **Organizational structure** — approval hierarchies, Compliance Officer identity, board/audit committee structure
4. **Risk appetite decisions** — whether to permit facilitation payments (most robust programs prohibit entirely), gift/hospitality thresholds (defaults provided below)
5. **Third-party landscape** — volume and type of agents, distributors, JV partners interacting with foreign officials

## Output Structure

Draft a policy with the following numbered sections:

### 1. Introduction & Scope

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Statutory basis | Anti-bribery: 15 U.S.C. §§ 78dd-1, -2, -3; Accounting: 15 U.S.C. §§ 78m(b)(2)(A)-(B) |
| Penalties | Criminal: up to $2M per violation (corporate); civil + disgorgement can far exceed; individual imprisonment up to 5 years [VERIFY current maximums post-inflation adjustments] |
| Enforcement authorities | DOJ (criminal), SEC (civil, issuers only) |
| Tone | Compliance = legal obligation + business integrity; employees who refuse corrupt practices are supported, not penalized |

### 2. Applicability

**Covered persons:** All employees, officers, directors, and agents globally — including foreign subsidiaries and non-U.S. nationals where any U.S. jurisdictional nexus exists.

**Jurisdictional nexus triggers** (examples):
- Emails routed through U.S.-based servers (Gmail, Microsoft 365)
- Wire transfers through U.S. correspondent banks
- Telephone calls to/from U.S.
- U.S. securities registration or periodic SEC reporting

**Third parties:** Policy binds agents, consultants, distributors, JV partners, customs brokers, freight forwarders acting on company's behalf. Willful blindness to third-party misconduct = liability.

**Conflict of laws:** Where local law conflicts with FCPA, comply with the more restrictive standard. Consult Legal/Compliance before proceeding.

---

### 3. Prohibited Conduct & Key Definitions

**Anti-bribery elements** — prohibits offering, promising, giving, or authorizing anything of value to a foreign official, directly or through intermediaries, corruptly to:
- Influence any act or decision in official capacity
- Induce violation of lawful duties
- Secure any improper advantage
- Obtain or retain business

**Key definitions:**

| Term | Scope |
|---|---|
| Foreign official | Government employees at all levels; officials of state-owned/controlled enterprises (telecoms, airlines, banks, oil companies — even minority government ownership); political party officials and candidates; employees of public international organizations (UN, World Bank, IMF, regional development banks) |
| Anything of value | Cash, gifts, meals, entertainment, travel, lodging, employment offers, charitable contributions, political donations, business opportunities, favorable contract terms |
| Issuer | Company with U.S.-registered securities or required to file periodic SEC reports |
| Domestic concern | U.S. citizens, nationals, residents; entities organized under U.S. law or with U.S. principal place of business |

**Facilitation payments:** [Policy election required] — Recommend **total prohibition**. The exception is narrow, difficult to document, prohibited by U.K. Bribery Act and many local laws, and creates compliance risk exceeding any benefit.

**Enforcement examples to include:** Consulting fees to officials' shell companies; luxury travel to influence procurement; hiring officials' relatives as quid pro quo; charity donations designated by official; cash payments to expedite customs clearance.

### 4. Gifts, Hospitality & Business Courtesies

**Permissibility criteria (all must be met):**
- [ ] Reasonable in value for the business relationship and local context
- [ ] Lawful under recipient's organization's policies and applicable local written law
- [ ] Directly related to legitimate business purpose (product demo, contract negotiation, training)
- [ ] Transparent and accurately documented in company records
- [ ] Not in cash; not provided secretly
- [ ] No expectation of specific official action in return
- [ ] Infrequent; no pattern suggesting improper influence

**Default thresholds (customize to company risk profile):**

| Category | Threshold | Approval Required |
|---|---|---|
| Single gift (foreign official) | ≤ $100 | None if within threshold |
| Aggregate gifts per recipient/year | ≤ $250 | None if within threshold |
| Meals (with business discussion) | Reasonable | Manager approval |
| Any courtesy exceeding thresholds | Any amount | Legal/Compliance written approval |
| Travel/lodging (business purpose) | Economy class; standard hotel | Legal/Compliance written approval |

**Prohibited regardless of amount:** Cash or cash equivalents; first-class/business-class travel (unless company policy applies equally to own employees); expenses for family members; personal/tourism side trips; tickets-only (no employee attendance) to sporting/cultural events.

**Approval request must include:** Business justification, recipient name/title/organization, description of courtesy, nexus to legitimate business purpose.

### 5. Third-Party Due Diligence

**Risk-tiered framework:**

| Tier | Risk Factors | Required Diligence |
|---|---|---|
| Low | No government interaction; low-risk jurisdiction; fixed-fee compensation | Business registration verification; sanctions/adverse media screening; FCPA compliance representation |
| Moderate | Occasional government contact; moderate-risk jurisdiction; commission-based comp | + Reference checks; qualifications assessment; compensation reasonableness review; review of third party's anti-corruption policies |
| High | Regular government interaction; high-risk jurisdiction (low TI CPI score); success-fee comp; government-recommended; government official ownership | + Third-party background investigation firm; ownership structure verification; site visit; in-person meeting with principals; compliance program audit; ongoing monitoring |

**High-risk triggers requiring enhanced diligence:**
- Operates in jurisdiction with TI Corruption Perceptions Index score below [threshold — recommend <50]
- Recommended by foreign official or government entity
- Commission contingent on government approvals
- Ownership includes government officials or their family members
- Lacks apparent qualifications or physical operations

**Required contractual provisions (all third-party agreements):**
- FCPA compliance representations and warranties
- Obligation to maintain accurate books and records
- Company audit rights
- Training obligations for personnel on company business
- Immediate termination right for violations or non-cooperation

**Compensation:** Must be reasonable and commensurate with documented services. Document market comparisons. Prohibit: round-sum payments, cash, offshore/third-country routing, compensation to parties lacking apparent capacity to perform.

**Ongoing monitoring:** Annual recertification; periodic transaction review; audit high-risk third parties; immediate investigation of red flags.

---

### 6. Accounting Provisions & Internal Controls

**Books and records mandate:** All transactions must be recorded accurately and in reasonable detail. Applies to all transactions — not limited to foreign-official interactions.

**Prohibited practices:**
- Off-books accounts or unrecorded funds
- False or misleading invoices, expense reports, or accounting entries
- Recording payments under generic or inaccurate descriptions

**Required internal controls:**

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| Segregation of duties | No single employee controls all aspects of high-risk transactions |
| Approval hierarchy | Management review for expenditures involving foreign officials or high-risk third parties |
| Expense flagging | Automated flags for unusual payment requests routed to Compliance before processing |
| Periodic audits | High-risk accounts and third-party transactions |
| Payment channel controls | Payments only to contracting party; only in country of service delivery |

**Finance red flags requiring escalation before payment:**
- Round-sum invoices without service detail
- Payments to third countries or offshore accounts
- Cash payment requests or unrelated-party routing
- Shell company or entity lacking apparent operations
- Unusual urgency without business justification
- Invoices from parties not in the approved vendor system

---

### 7. Training Requirements

| Population | Frequency | Content |
|---|---|---|
| All employees (international ops, finance) | At hire; annually | FCPA overview, definitions, red flags, reporting channels |
| High-risk populations (sales, BD, procurement, finance in high-risk markets) | At hire; annually; role change | Enhanced: complex scenarios, approval workflows, due diligence tools |
| Senior management | Annually | Culture of compliance, resource adequacy, escalation obligations |
| Board/Audit Committee | Annually | Oversight responsibilities, key risks, program effectiveness |
| High-risk third parties | Contractually required | FCPA fundamentals; company policy obligations |

**Documentation:** Written certification of completion and understanding. Maintain records demonstrating comprehension (assessments/quizzes).

---

### 8. Reporting & Non-Retaliation

**Reporting channels:**
- 24/7 multilingual compliance hotline (anonymous where legally permitted)
- Web-based reporting portal
- Direct access to Legal/Compliance Department

**Investigation commitments:** Prompt review; qualified/independent investigators; documentation preservation; escalation to senior management and Audit Committee for significant matters; feedback to reporters where possible.

**Non-retaliation:** Adverse employment actions prohibited for good-faith reporters, investigation participants, or employees who refuse to participate in potential violations. Retaliation = independent disciplinable offense through termination.

**Applicable legal protections:**
- Dodd-Frank Act: Financial incentives and anti-retaliation for SEC reporters [VERIFY current bounty percentages]
- Sarbanes-Oxley Act: Anti-retaliation for employees of public companies reporting fraud

---

### 9. Enforcement & Accountability

**Discipline:** Violations subject to termination regardless of seniority, tenure, or business outcome. May also include demotion, suspension, forfeiture of bonus/equity.

**Personal liability notice:** Company cannot indemnify employees for personal criminal fines. Individual exposure includes imprisonment (up to 5 years), personal fines, civil penalties, disgorgement, professional debarment.

**Government cooperation:** Company reserves right to voluntary self-disclosure. Cooperation and remediation are significant factors in DOJ/SEC charging decisions.

**Program governance:**
- Designated Chief Compliance Officer with board/audit committee reporting line
- Annual policy review and risk assessment
- Compliance Officer has authority to escalate without management interference
- Adequate budget and staffing for training, diligence, monitoring, and investigations

## Guidelines

- **Verify citations:** Confirm current penalty amounts annually — subject to Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act
- **Facilitation payments:** Default to total prohibition; document policy election and rationale
- **Thresholds:** Calibrate gift/hospitality limits to company's specific risk profile and industry norms; document basis
- **U.K. Bribery Act overlap:** Flag where company's U.K. nexus triggers stricter standards (e.g., no facilitation payment exception, adequate procedures defense)
- **State-owned enterprise analysis:** Always assess government ownership stake in counterparty's employer — even minority ownership can qualify employees as foreign officials
- **Local law conflicts:** Apply more restrictive standard; document analysis
- **Authenticity over formalism:** DOJ/SEC evaluate whether program is "operationalized" — paper policy without training, monitoring, and enforcement is insufficient
- **Do not** speculate on whether specific historical conduct by the company violated the FCPA without attorney review

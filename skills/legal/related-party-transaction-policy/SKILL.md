# Related Party Transaction Policy

Drafts a board-adoptable Related Party Transaction Policy governing identification, Audit Committee review, approval, and SEC-compliant disclosure of related party transactions.

## Prerequisites

1. **Company profile** — legal name, state of incorporation, public/private status, exchange listing (NYSE/Nasdaq)
2. **Governance documents** — certificate of incorporation, bylaws, Audit Committee charter, existing Code of Ethics or Corporate Governance Guidelines
3. **Existing RPTs** — any current or anticipated related party relationships or transactions
4. **Dollar threshold preference** — internal approval threshold (common: $120K, $250K, or $500K)

## Output Structure

### 1. Header & Purpose
- Full title: `[Corporation Legal Name] Related Party Transaction Policy and Procedures`
- Purpose: transparency, conflict prevention, SEC compliance, shareholder protection, and enabling legitimate transactions with proper safeguards
- Effective date, version number, adoption authority (full Board)

### 2. Definitions

| Term | Core Definition |
|---|---|
| **Related Party** | Directors, executive officers, director nominees, >5% beneficial owners of any voting class, and their immediate family members |
| **Immediate Family Member** | Spouse, parents, children, siblings, in-laws (mothers/fathers/sons/daughters/brothers/sisters), household members (non-tenant/employee) |
| **Related Party Transaction** | Any transaction/arrangement where (a) aggregate amount exceeds the threshold, (b) company or subsidiary is a participant, and (c) a Related Party has a direct or indirect material interest |
| **Material Interest** | Excludes: position as director/officer of company; <10% ownership of a counterparty entity |

> **Threshold calibration**: SEC disclosure threshold is $120,000 (Item 404(a) of Reg. S-K). Public companies commonly adopt $120K for disclosure and a higher internal threshold ($250K–$500K) for pre-approval triggers. Align definitions to capture all Item 404(a) persons.

**Covered transaction examples** (illustrative, non-exhaustive):
- Purchases/sales of property or services
- Consulting or employment arrangements outside ordinary course
- Loans or guarantees of indebtedness
- Charitable contributions where Related Party is an executive officer or director
- Any transaction where Related Party interest could influence company judgment

### 3. Identification Procedures

**Individual obligations:**
- All directors and executive officers must promptly disclose any potential RPT to the Audit Committee Chair and General Counsel before any binding commitment

**Institutional monitoring:**
- Legal, Finance, and Internal Audit implement controls covering: vendor relationships, significant contracts, expense reports, investment activities, charitable contribution requests
- Annual questionnaires to all directors and executive officers disclosing related party relationships and potential transactions

### 4. Audit Committee Review & Approval

**Authority:** Audit Committee has exclusive approval authority; interested parties must recuse from all deliberations and voting.

**Required submission materials:**
- Description and material terms of the transaction
- Related Party's nature of interest
- Business purpose and anticipated company benefit
- Ordinary course analysis
- Comparable arm's-length terms (if available)
- Any other information the Committee requests

**Approval standard:** Committee must determine in good faith that the transaction is **fair and reasonable to the company** and in the best interests of the company and shareholders.

**Ongoing transactions:** Annual re-review required; Committee may establish standing guidelines for recurring arrangements.

### 5. Pre-Approved Categories (Standing Approval)

| Category | Condition |
|---|---|
| Executive officer employment/compensation | Approved by Board or Compensation Committee per standard procedures |
| Director compensation | Approved by full Board |
| Related Party as non-executive employee of counterparty | <10% equity ownership in counterparty |
| Broad-based employee benefit plans | Available to all employees on same terms |
| De minimis transactions | Below threshold set by Audit Committee |

### 6. Disclosure Obligations (Public Companies)

- All RPTs meeting SEC thresholds disclosed per **Item 404 of Regulation S-K** in: proxy statements, Form 10-K, Form 10-Q, Form 8-K (as applicable)
- General Counsel and CFO responsible for accuracy, completeness, and timeliness
- Audit Committee reviews all RPT disclosures before inclusion in SEC filings
- Consider full Board reporting of all RPTs regardless of public disclosure requirement

### 7. Administration & Enforcement

| Topic | Provision |
|---|---|
| Policy administrator | Audit Committee |
| Annual review | Audit Committee reassesses; recommends amendments to full Board |
| Material amendments | Require full Board approval; disclose per applicable law and exchange rules |
| Pre-effective date transactions | Specify whether ongoing transactions are captured |
| Non-compliance consequences | Disciplinary action up to termination; unapproved transactions subject to rescission, modification, or ratification; disgorgement of profits |

### 8. Document Formatting

- Numbered sections and subsections with descriptive headings
- Page headers: company name + policy title; footers: page numbers
- Adoption date, version number, Audit Committee Chair and Corporate Secretary signature lines
- Cross-references to Audit Committee charter, Code of Ethics, Corporate Governance Guidelines
- Target length: 4–7 pages

## Guidelines

- **Public vs. private**: SEC disclosure obligations (Item 404, Reg. S-K) apply only to public reporting companies; private companies should still adopt substantive approval procedures but tailor disclosure provisions accordingly
- **Exchange rules**: Nasdaq Rule 5630 and NYSE Listed Company Manual §314 impose independent director approval requirements for RPTs — verify current exchange requirements [VERIFY specific rule numbers against current exchange manuals]
- **State law**: Delaware and most states impose fiduciary duty overlay on interested transactions; entire fairness standard may apply if approval procedures are not followed
- **Definition expansion**: Consider extending Related Party definition to entities where covered persons hold ≥10% equity or serve as executive officer/general partner, depending on company risk profile
- **Consistency**: Reconcile terminology and authority with existing Audit Committee charter — policy must not create conflicting governance authority
- **Ratification**: Address whether Audit Committee can ratify transactions entered into without prior approval and under what conditions

# Deposition Objection Reference

Quick reference for making, preserving, and responding to objections under FRCP 30(c)(2), for both taking and defending depositions.

## Prerequisites

1. Identify whether you are **taking** or **defending** the deposition
2. Know the applicable court (federal FRCP vs. state rules — state rules vary)
3. For 30(b)(6): have the notice of deposition topics available
4. For privilege assertions: identify privileged areas and any applicable protective orders in advance

## Fundamental Rules

| Rule | Summary |
|------|---------|
| Witness still answers | Most objections preserve the record only; witness answers unless instructed not to |
| Concise objections required | FRCP 30(c)(2): "stated concisely in a nonargumentative and nonsuggestive manner" |
| Form objections are waived | Must be made at the deposition or they're lost |
| Instructions not to answer are rare | Only 3 valid grounds under FRCP 30(c)(2) |

**Proper:** `"Objection, form."` / `"Objection, leading."` / `"Objection, assumes facts."`  
**Improper:** `"Objection, that question is confusing and compound and assumes facts..."` (speaking objection)  
**Improper:** `"Objection — the witness already told you he doesn't remember that."` (coaching)

---

## Form Objections (Waived If Not Made at Deposition)

| Objection | When to Use | Phrasing |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Compound** | Question asks two or more things at once | `"Objection, compound."` |
| **Leading** | Question suggests the answer; most relevant when defending your own witness | `"Objection, leading."` |
| **Assumes facts not in evidence** | Question presupposes an unestablished fact (e.g., "After you deleted the emails...") | `"Objection, assumes facts."` |
| **Vague / Ambiguous** | Unclear referent or undefined term | `"Objection, vague."` |
| **Unintelligible** | Question doesn't parse | `"Objection, unintelligible."` |
| **Calls for speculation** | Asks witness to guess at others' thoughts or counterfactuals | `"Objection, speculation."` |
| **Calls for narrative** | Overly broad question inviting unstructured speech; use sparingly | `"Objection, narrative."` |
| **Mischaracterizes testimony / misstates evidence** | Question inaccurately summarizes prior testimony or a document | `"Objection, mischaracterizes the witness's testimony."` |
| **Argumentative** | Question debates rather than seeks facts | `"Objection, argumentative."` |
| **Asked and answered** | Same question already fully answered; not mere clarification | `"Objection, asked and answered."` |
| **Lacks foundation** | Witness's personal knowledge not established | `"Objection, foundation."` |

---

## Substantive Objections (Not Waived — Preserved for Trial)

| Objection | Notes | Phrasing |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| **Relevance** | Threshold is low in discovery (FRCP 26(b)(1)); don't overuse | `"Objection, relevance."` |
| **Hearsay** | Witness still answers; admissibility decided at trial | `"Objection, hearsay."` |
| **Privilege** | May warrant instruction not to answer — see below | `"Objection, attorney-client privilege."` [specify] |
| **Beyond scope (30(b)(6))** | Question exceeds noticed topics; witness may still answer on personal knowledge | `"Objection, beyond the scope of the noticed topics."` |

## Instructions Not to Answer — FRCP 30(c)(2)

> *"A person may instruct a deponent not to answer only when necessary to preserve a privilege, to enforce a limitation ordered by the court, or to present a motion under Rule 30(d)(3)."*

### Ground 1: Privilege

**When proper:** Question seeks attorney-client communication, work product, or other recognized privilege (spousal, doctor-patient, priest-penitent).

**Procedure:**
1. State the privilege on the record
2. Instruct the witness not to answer
3. Briefly state the basis
4. Be prepared to log on a privilege log if demanded

**Example:**  
Q: *"What did your attorney advise you to do about the contract?"*  
A: `"Objection, attorney-client privilege. I instruct the witness not to answer. The question seeks communications made for the purpose of obtaining legal advice."`

**Caution:** Privilege is waived by prior voluntary disclosure or by presence of third parties.

---

### Ground 2: Court-Ordered Limitation

**When proper:** A protective order or court order restricts the topic or manner of deposition.

**Example:**  
`"Objection. The Court's protective order dated [date] prohibits inquiry into the manufacturing process. I instruct the witness not to answer."`

---

### Ground 3: Motion to Terminate — Bad Faith / Harassment (Rule 30(d)(3))

**When proper:** Questioning is conducted in bad faith or designed to harass, embarrass, or oppress.

**Procedure:**
1. State on the record you are suspending to seek a protective order
2. Promptly file under Rule 30(d)(3)
3. If court disagrees, sanctions may follow — use this only when conduct is egregious

**Example:**  
`"Your questions are designed to harass the witness, not seek relevant information. I am suspending this deposition to file a motion under Rule 30(d)(3)."`

---

### Improper Grounds for Instruction Not to Answer

❌ Relevance alone  
❌ Embarrassment (unless harassment threshold met)  
❌ Answer is harmful to your case  
❌ Witness claims not to know (answer: "I don't know")  
❌ Form defects (compound, vague, leading — object, but witness answers)  
❌ Hearsay  
❌ Beyond scope in a regular (non-30(b)(6)) deposition

## Defending Counsel Pre-Deposition Checklist

- [ ] Identify privilege areas; brief witness that you may instruct not to answer
- [ ] Review any protective orders; know exactly what topics are restricted
- [ ] Prepare witness: your objections don't signal how to answer
- [ ] Identify likely mischaracterization risks (prior testimony, key documents)
- [ ] Determine whether conduct risks warrant having Rule 30(d)(3) papers ready
- [ ] For 30(b)(6): map noticed topics; designate scope on the record

## Guidelines

- **Never coach through objections** — adding substance after "objection" (e.g., "...and the witness should only testify to what he personally knows") is sanctionable
- **Don't over-object** — excessive objections signal importance to opposing counsel and can appear obstructionist
- **State your ground when instructing not to answer** — bare "don't answer" instructions invite disputes and court scrutiny
- **State court practice varies** — some states (e.g., California, New York) have specific deposition conduct rules that differ from FRCP; verify local rules
- ***Hall v. Clifton Precision*, 150 F.R.D. 525 (E.D. Pa. 1993)** — seminal case on speaking objections and coaching [VERIFY citation still good law in your jurisdiction]
- **FRCP 32(d)(3)** — statutory basis for waiver of form objections not made at deposition

# Legal Memo & Brief Drafting

Drafts structured legal documents using research, analysis, and persuasive reasoning.

## Prerequisites

1. **Assignment** — the legal question, audience (internal memo vs. court filing), and any page/format constraints
2. **Research** — completed legal research with relevant authorities identified
3. **Facts** — relevant facts of the matter, organized chronologically

## Workflow

### 1. Structure the Document

**For internal memos:** Question Presented → Short Answer → Facts → Discussion → Conclusion

**For briefs:** Introduction → Statement of Facts → Argument (organized by issue) → Conclusion

### 2. Draft Each Section

- **Facts** — neutral, chronological, include only legally relevant facts; cite record where applicable
- **Analysis/Argument** — IRAC structure per issue (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion)
- **Citations** — proper format for jurisdiction; pin cites to specific pages

### 3. Strengthen Persuasion

- Lead with strongest arguments
- Address and distinguish adverse authority
- Use topic sentences that state conclusions
- Ensure logical flow between sections

### 4. Review and Polish

- Verify all citations are accurate and current
- Check compliance with court rules (page limits, formatting, filing requirements)
- Ensure consistent terminology throughout
- Store final version in vault

## Pitfalls

- **Don't bury the conclusion** — state your answer upfront, then support it
- **Cite everything** — unsupported legal assertions destroy credibility
- **Address counterarguments** — ignoring them doesn't make them go away
- **Match tone to audience** — internal memos should be objective; briefs should advocate

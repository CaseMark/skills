# Revocable Living Trust

Drafts a revocable living trust that preserves grantor control during lifetime and provides seamless asset transfer at death, tailored to applicable state law.

## Prerequisites

1. **Client information** — grantor(s) full legal name, address, state of residence, marital status, individual vs. joint trust
2. **Beneficiary details** — primary and contingent beneficiaries with full names, relationships, distribution preferences (per stirpes/per capita), age-based restrictions for minors
3. **Asset inventory** — real property (legal descriptions, APNs), financial accounts, business interests, significant personal property
4. **Trustee selections** — initial trustee, first/second/third successor trustees, any co-trustee arrangements, corporate trustee preferences
5. **Distribution plan** — specific bequests, age-based schedules for minors, continued trust vs. outright distribution, spendthrift needs
6. **State-specific requirements** — execution formalities, community property vs. common law, applicable trust code provisions

## Output Structure

### 1. Declaration of Trust

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Trust name | Grantor name + year (e.g., "The John Smith 2026 Revocable Living Trust") |
| Grantor ID | Full legal name, address |
| Effective date | Date of execution |
| Initial trustee | Typically the grantor; both spouses for joint trusts |
| Trust type | Individual or joint; explicit revocability statement |
| Capacity statement | Voluntary intent, legal capacity |
| Primary purposes | Lifetime asset management + death distribution |
| Residence/jurisdiction | Grantor's state of residence |

### 2. Trust Property and Funding

Schedule of assets as exhibit, organized by category:

- **Real property** — full legal description, APN, address
- **Financial accounts** — bank, brokerage, retirement (flag qualified plan restrictions)
- **Business interests** — entity name, ownership percentage, entity type
- **Personal property** — vehicles, valuables, collectibles
- **Future funding** — language permitting ongoing funding without formal amendment; mechanism for adding/removing assets

### 3. Trustee Provisions

```
Initial Trustee:        [Name, Address]
First Successor:        [Name, Address]
Second Successor:       [Name, Address]
Third Successor/Corp:   [Name or institution]
```

Address each:

- [ ] Incapacity definition and determination process (physician certification standard)
- [ ] Triggering conditions: death, resignation, incapacity, removal
- [ ] Co-trustee decision-making protocol (if applicable)
- [ ] Written acceptance requirement
- [ ] Bond waiver (typical for family members)
- [ ] Corporate trustee authorization and special provisions
- [ ] Resignation procedure and replacement mechanism

### 4. Beneficiary Designations and Distributions

**Lifetime distributions:** Unrestricted income and principal to grantor.

**Post-death distributions:**

| Beneficiary | Relationship | Share | Distribution Type | Conditions |
|-------------|-------------|-------|-------------------|------------|
| [Name] | [Relation] | [%] | Outright / In trust | [Age triggers, etc.] |

Include:

- [ ] Per stirpes vs. per capita designation
- [ ] Specific bequests of identified property
- [ ] Minor beneficiary provisions — age-based schedule (e.g., 1/3 at 25, 1/3 at 30, balance at 35)
- [ ] Spendthrift clause
- [ ] Contingent/residuary beneficiaries
- [ ] Ultimate disposition if all beneficiaries predecease

### 5. Revocation and Amendment

- Grantor retains absolute authority while living and competent
- Amendments: written, signed; specify notarization/witness requirements per state law
- Partial amendments (modify provisions) or full restatement permitted
- Revocation returns all property to grantor absolutely
- **Joint trust:** specify whether both spouses must consent; what happens at first death (irrevocable vs. amendable by survivor)
- Trust becomes irrevocable at grantor's death or incapacity

### 6. Trustee Powers

Grant comprehensive authority including:

| Category | Powers |
|----------|--------|
| Investment | Any asset class; retain original assets; vote shares; manage business interests |
| Management | Sell, exchange, lease, mortgage trust property |
| Distribution | HEMS standard or broader discretionary standard (specify which) |
| Administrative | Hire professionals, delegate functions, maintain insurance, handle taxes, keep records |
| Special assets | Continue business operations, manage rental property, handle IP |

Also address:

- Standard of care: prudent investor rule (or state-specific standard)
- Compensation: reasonable fees for professionals; family trustee fee arrangement
- Liability and indemnification provisions
- Removal provisions

### 7. General Provisions

- [ ] Governing law designation (grantor's state of residence)
- [ ] Severability clause
- [ ] No-contest / in terrorem clause
- [ ] Spendthrift provisions
- [ ] Tax allocation and payment directives
- [ ] Defined terms: "issue," "descendants," "incapacity," "per stirpes"
- [ ] Accounting duty — frequency, waiver provisions
- [ ] Anti-merger provision (sole trustee + sole beneficiary scenario)
- [ ] Trust situs and jurisdictional court
- [ ] Survivor requirements (e.g., 30-day survivorship)

### 8. Execution

- [ ] Signature blocks for grantor(s) and trustee(s)
- [ ] Notarization (required in most states; verify state-specific rules)
- [ ] Witness signatures if state requires
- [ ] Asset schedule exhibit attached (even if initially blank)
- [ ] Consistent trust name throughout; verify all cross-references
- [ ] Pagination and professional formatting

## Guidelines

- **State law controls** — trust execution requirements, community property rules, trust code provisions, and permissible trustee powers vary significantly by state. Always confirm applicable state requirements.
- **Unfunded trusts are ineffective** — emphasize the funding schedule and provide instructions for retitling assets.
- **Coordinate with pour-over will** — note that a companion pour-over will is typically needed to capture assets not transferred during lifetime.
- **Qualified plan limitations** — retirement accounts cannot be retitled into a trust during lifetime; the trust can be named as beneficiary, but flag tax implications.
- **HEMS vs. broad discretion** — use HEMS standard for ascertainable standard (avoids estate inclusion for non-grantor trustees); use broader discretion only where grantor is trustee or inclusion is acceptable.
- **Joint trust considerations** — clearly delineate community vs. separate property in community property states; address A/B trust split or disclaimer trust if tax planning is relevant.
- **Do not provide tax advice** — flag estate tax, generation-skipping transfer tax, and income tax issues for CPA/tax counsel review.
- **Mark uncertain citations** with `[VERIFY]` — state-specific statutes, UTC section references, and case law should be verified against current authority.

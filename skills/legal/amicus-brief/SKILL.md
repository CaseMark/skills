# Amicus Brief

Produce or evaluate U.S. appellate amicus briefs that add a distinct, rule-compliant perspective to the court.

## Prerequisites

1. Case posture and issues presented, plus parties’ briefs or at minimum their arguments and requested relief
2. Amicus identity, constituency, and concrete stake in the outcome
3. Applicable court rules (FRAP 29, state equivalents, or Supreme Court Rule 37) and local formatting requirements
4. Consent status or plan for motion for leave, plus filing deadline
5. Source set for legal authorities and any empirical or policy materials to be cited

## Output Structure / Process

Mode selection:

| Mode | Use when | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Drafting | Creating a new amicus brief | Full brief with required sections and compliance items |
| Analysis | Reviewing an existing amicus brief | Structured critique and summary table |

Drafting workflow checklist:

- Confirm jurisdiction and rule set (federal, state, Supreme Court)
- Verify consent or prepare motion for leave
- Identify unique contribution and avoid duplicating party arguments
- Build authority map: controlling precedent, persuasive precedent, statutes, regulations, secondary sources
- Integrate expertise or policy evidence with clear sourcing
- Draft sections in required order and verify page/word limits
- Add disclosure statements, certificates, and signatures
- Run compliance pass: Bluebook, formatting, service, and ECF rules

Analysis workflow checklist:

- Identify distinct contribution and non-duplication of party arguments
- Assess credibility and fit of amicus interest
- Evaluate legal authority strength and accuracy of citations
- Flag any new issues not raised by parties
- Check compliance with rule requirements and formatting
- Summarize core arguments and practical or policy impacts

Required sections by default (adapt to court rules):

- Table of Contents
- Table of Authorities
- Statement of Interest
- Summary of Argument
- Argument with headings
- Conclusion
- Certificates and disclosures (see rule checks)

Rule checks (confirm for the specific court):

| Requirement | What to confirm |
| --- | --- |
| Consent or leave | Parties’ consent filed or motion for leave prepared |
| Timing | Filing deadline, often within 7 days after the supported party’s principal brief [VERIFY] |
| Length | Word/page limits for amicus briefs [VERIFY] |
| Disclosure | Authorship and funding disclosure (FRAP 29(a)(4)(E); Supreme Court Rule 37.6) |
| Corporate disclosure | Entity disclosure statement if required (FRAP 26.1 or local equivalent) |
| Service | Proper service on all parties and proof of service |
| Format | Font, spacing, margins, cover color, ECF requirements |

Argument structure guidance (use headings; keep tight and specific):

| Section | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Interest | Establish legitimacy and why the amicus perspective matters |
| Summary | One-page max, stating the unique thesis and outcome requested |
| Argument | 2–4 core points, each tied to controlling law and practical impact |
| Conclusion | Clear request for disposition or adoption of rule |

Template outline:

```text
[Cover / Caption]

TABLE OF CONTENTS
TABLE OF AUTHORITIES

STATEMENT OF INTEREST
[Explain amicus constituency, expertise, and direct stake.]

CONSENT / LEAVE STATUS
[State consent or note motion for leave.]

SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT
[Concise thesis and outcome sought.]

ARGUMENT
I. [Point 1: Legal principle + unique perspective]
II. [Point 2: Policy/industry/technical evidence tied to law]
III. [Point 3: Practical consequences or administrability]

CONCLUSION
[Requested disposition.]

DISCLOSURE OF AUTHORSHIP AND FUNDING
[FRAP 29(a)(4)(E) / Supreme Court Rule 37.6 statement.]

CORPORATE DISCLOSURE STATEMENT
[If required by court rule.]

CERTIFICATE OF COMPLIANCE
[Word count method and total.]

CERTIFICATE OF SERVICE
[Service method, date, recipients.]
```

## Guidelines

- Do not duplicate party briefs; add distinct law, data, or consequences
- Avoid introducing new issues; if referencing extra-record materials, frame as legislative facts or policy context
- Use Bluebook citations and verify every authority for accuracy
- Keep tone neutral, precise, and judicially useful; avoid advocacy excess
- State the amicus interest concretely and tie it to the outcome sought
- Follow jurisdictional rules first; FRAP/SCOTUS rules are defaults only
- Redact or avoid confidential information not in the public record

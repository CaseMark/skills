# Time-Limited Policy Limits Demand

## Why This Skill Exists

A time-limited demand is the single most powerful instrument in plaintiff-side insurance litigation. Done correctly, it creates an irrefutable record that the insurer had a reasonable opportunity to protect its insured from excess exposure — and failed. Done poorly, it becomes a trap for the plaintiff: ambiguous acceptance mechanics let the insurer claim it "tried to accept," impossible conditions give the insurer a defense, and sloppy liability narratives undermine the reasonableness of the demand itself. In states like Georgia (O.C.G.A. § 9-11-67.1), strict statutory requirements mean a single procedural defect can destroy the bad faith claim entirely.

This skill produces a defensible, time-limited settlement demand with clear acceptance mechanics, sourced damages, and jurisdiction-aware bad faith framing.

---

## Checkpoint A: Pre-Draft Intake (Mandatory)

Ask every time unless the user says "use defaults" or "just draft." Gather:

1. **Jurisdiction and claim posture** — governing state; pre-suit, in suit, pre/post discovery
2. **Insurer and insured identifiers** — claim number, policy number if known, adjuster contact, all potential insureds/entities
3. **Liability evidence** — reports, photos/video, witness statements, citations
4. **Damages proof** — medical records/bills, wage loss, prognosis, future care, impairment
5. **Policy limits** — confirmed amount or plan to request confirmation
6. **Lien status** — health insurer, Medicare/Medicaid, ERISA, hospital, workers' comp
7. **Settlement authority** — confirmed from client on amount and release scope

**If the user doesn't respond**, apply and clearly label these defaults: pre-suit posture; full policy limits demand; standard release of named insured for bodily injury claims; response window marked `[VERIFY JURISDICTION]` (do not default to a fixed number of days); professional notice tone.

If any core item is missing (especially settlement authority and policy limits), stop and request it before drafting.

---

## Step 1: Map Jurisdictional Requirements

- Identify legal theory: common-law failure-to-settle, statutory bad faith, unfair claims practices
- Verify notice/cure requirements, service methods, and required content for the state
- Flag all legal statements without a verified URL as `[VERIFY]`
- If a statutory notice is required (e.g., FL Civ. Remedy Notice), draft it separately or integrate only if the statute allows

---

## Step 2: Draft Liability Narrative

- 5–10 sentences max, evidence-cited
- Address obvious defenses and comparative fault
- Tie liability clarity to foreseeable excess exposure
- Do not overstate — the narrative must be defensible if read to a jury

---

## Step 3: Present Damages Exposure

- Provide itemized specials with documentation references
- Describe permanency and future care with source support
- Include a plausible verdict range rationale; mark any verdict research as `[VERIFY]` if not sourced
- Ground the excess-exposure argument in actual numbers, not conclusory statements

---

## Step 4: Structure Offer Terms

- Clear, unequivocal offer within limits or specified amount
- Define release scope, parties released, and claims reserved
- Define payment mechanics: payee, delivery method, timing
- **Acceptance must be objectively measurable** — no ambiguity about what constitutes acceptance

---

## Step 5: Set Deadline

- Specific date, time, and time zone
- Brief reason for the window's reasonableness
- State automatic withdrawal if not accepted exactly as specified
- Align with any statutory minimum response periods

---

## Step 6: Frame Bad Faith Exposure

- Professional notice tone — no threats, no inflammatory language
- State excess exposure risk and insurer's duty to protect insured
- Request that insurer communicate the demand to the insured
- Do not use language that could be characterized as coercive

---

## Step 7: Specify Delivery and Record-Keeping

- Specify delivery channels per jurisdictional requirements
- Require written acceptance to a tracked inbox
- Attach exhibit index and label all attachments
- Preserve proof of delivery and attachments

### Required Sections in the Letter

- Caption block with parties, claim number, policy number
- Settlement offer and acceptance mechanics
- Liability summary with exhibit references
- Damages summary with totals
- Deadline and withdrawal statement
- Release scope summary
- Lien/Medicare handling statement
- Delivery instructions
- Exhibit index

### Letter Template

```text
[Date]

Via [email + certified/overnight if required]
[Adjuster Name]
[Insurer]
[Address]
Re: [Claimant] v. [Insured]
Claim No.: [ ]
Policy No.: [ ]
Loss Date: [ ]

This is a time-limited policy-limits demand to protect your insured from excess exposure.

Liability
- [2–4 sentences with exhibit references]

Damages
- Specials to date: $[ ] (Ex. [ ])
- Future care/prognosis: [ ] (Ex. [ ])
- Wage loss: $[ ] (Ex. [ ])
- Summary: A reasonable jury could return a verdict exceeding policy limits.

Offer
In exchange for tender of the full and complete liability limits of $[ ] under
Policy No. [ ], [Claimant] will execute a release of [Named Insured(s)] for all
bodily injury claims arising from the [date] incident. The release does not
include [reserved claims/parties].

Acceptance
Written acceptance must be received by [date], [time], [time zone] at [email].
Payment must be issued by that deadline and sent via [overnight/wire] to:
Payee: "[Client Name] and [Law Firm] Client Trust Account"
Address: [ ]

Liens
[Claimant] will satisfy valid liens from settlement proceeds. If Medicare/ERISA
applies, [state escrow/holdback or cooperation language].

Deadline
This offer expires automatically if not accepted exactly as stated by the
deadline above.

Notice
Given clear liability and damages, this demand provides a reasonable opportunity
to protect the insured from excess exposure. Please provide a copy to your
insured so they may seek independent counsel.

Attachments
Exhibit Index: [list]
```

---

## Step 8: Run Quality Control

### Acceptance Checklist

- Written acceptance received by deadline
- Amount matches policy limits or stated sum
- All insureds covered match release scope
- Payment method and payees are correct
- No added conditions; if added, treat as counteroffer

---

## Checkpoint B: Post-Draft Alignment (Mandatory)

After delivering the initial draft, ask:

1. Are the policy limits confirmed, or do we need to request confirmation before sending?
2. Does the release scope correctly identify all parties to be released and all claims reserved?
3. Is the deadline window reasonable given the jurisdiction's requirements and case posture?
4. Are there any statutory notice requirements (e.g., FL Civil Remedy Notice, GA § 9-11-67.1) that need a separate filing?

If the user doesn't answer, recommend confirming policy limits and release scope (most common failure points) and proceed if authorized.

## Guidelines

**Jurisdiction Flags (verify before use):**
- **Texas**: Stowers elements `[VERIFY]` (*G.A. Stowers Furniture Co. v. American Indemnity Co.*, 15 S.W.2d 544 (Tex. 1929))
- **California**: prudent-insurer standard `[VERIFY]` (*Comunale*, *Crisci*), unknown-claims waiver (Cal. Civ. Code § 1542 `[VERIFY]`)
- **Georgia**: strict statutory TLD requirements and service methods (O.C.G.A. § 9-11-67.1 `[VERIFY]`)
- **Florida**: statutory bad faith and civil remedy notice (Fla. Stat. § 624.155 `[VERIFY]`), tort reform changes `[VERIFY]`

**Anti-Hallucination:**
- Do not assert legal standards or statutory duties without a verified source; mark `[VERIFY]` if uncertain
- Do not misstate limits, injuries, or specials; reconcile with exhibits
- Do not invent verdict data or "average settlement values"
- Do not fabricate case citations

**Ethics:**
- Avoid impossible conditions, hidden terms, or ambiguous acceptance steps
- Keep tone professional; no threats to report adjusters or criminal allegations
- Do not communicate directly with represented insureds without attorney approval
- Protect client confidentiality; disclose only necessary medical detail

**ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED** — Always require attorney review before sending.

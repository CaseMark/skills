# Pet Custody Best Interest Analysis

## Why This Skill Exists

Companion animal disputes in dissolutions are one of the fastest-evolving areas of family law, with a patchwork of statutes (CA, IL, NY, AK) creating genuine best-interest standards while most jurisdictions still treat pets as personal property. Attorneys who apply the wrong framework — or import child custody terminology into a property-only jurisdiction — undermine credibility with the court. Equally dangerous is failing to distinguish legal ownership from primary caregiving from de facto control, since each thread carries different evidentiary weight depending on the forum. This skill produces an evidence-grounded, factor-by-factor analysis that maps to the correct legal standard and anticipates adversarial attacks.

---

## Checkpoint A: Pre-Draft Intake (Mandatory)

Ask every time unless the user says "use defaults" or "just draft." Gather:

1. **Forum identification** — state, court level, and proceeding type (divorce, domestic partnership dissolution, cohabitation breakup, civil dispute)
2. **Procedural posture** — temporary relief vs. final allocation; scheduling order and deadlines
3. **Core case documents** — petition/complaint, responses, temporary orders, property schedules listing the pet, mediation statements
4. **Ownership records** — adoption/purchase agreements, microchip registration, municipal licenses, breeder agreements
5. **Veterinary records** — complete history (minimum 3 years), identifying who attended appointments
6. **Financial records** — payments for food, grooming, insurance, emergency care, boarding; whose name on accounts
7. **Caretaking evidence** — daily routine logs, calendar entries, texts discussing pet-care duties, pet-sitting app records
8. **Living environment** — current and proposed residences, photos, lease/HOA pet policies, proximity to parks and vet clinics, daily schedule, travel frequency
9. **Emotional bond evidence** — third-party affidavits (neighbors, trainers, groomers), timestamped photos/videos, behavioral observations
10. **Pet profile** — age, breed, temperament, medical conditions, training level, anxiety triggers
11. **Safety concerns** — allegations of neglect, abuse, DV, threats involving the pet, interference with access

**If the user doesn't respond**, apply and clearly label these defaults: well-being statute analysis if jurisdiction has one, otherwise hybrid equitable; attorney work product audience; final allocation posture. Mark any assumed facts with [ASSUMED].

## Step 1: Determine Governing Legal Standard

Classify the forum into one of three modes:

| Mode | Frame | Key Evidence |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Well-being statute** | Animal welfare factors control | Caregiving, stability, attachment, medical management |
| **Hybrid equitable** | Property with equitable discretion | Ownership + welfare as equitable considerations |
| **Pure property** | Ownership and equitable distribution | Title, purchase, registration, financial contribution |

**Key statutes** (all [VERIFY] for current amendments and applicability):

| Jurisdiction | Statute | Standard |
|-------------|---------|----------|
| California | Fam. Code § 2605 | Sole/joint ownership considering care of the pet |
| Illinois | 750 ILCS 5/503(n) | Well-being of companion animal |
| New York | DRL § 236(B)(5)(d)(15) | Best interest of companion animal |
| Alaska | AS 25.24.160(a)(5) | Well-being of the animal |

- In property-only jurisdictions (e.g., TX, FL): use "allocation," "possession," or "award" — not "custody." Frame welfare facts as evidence of intrinsic value to the possessor.
- Verify every statutory and case citation via web search or mark [VERIFY].
- Do not assume availability of pet visitation orders, pet support, or enforcement mechanisms without verified authority.

---

## Step 2: Build the Fact Record

Produce three distinct evidentiary threads — do not conflate them:

| Thread | Definition | Key Evidence |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Legal ownership** | Contractual title and registration | Adoption/purchase docs, microchip, license |
| **Primary caregiving** | Who performs daily non-discretionary care and medical decision-making | Vet records, pharmacy receipts, feeding/walking logs, training records |
| **De facto control** | Who has possessed the pet since separation and how stable the arrangement has been | Post-separation timeline, interference allegations |

Construct a chronological timeline from acquisition → separation → present. Flag gaps and propose targeted discovery (subpoenas, declarations, interrogatories) for unsupported assertions.

---

## Step 3: Apply Best-Interests Factors

Analyze each factor with neutral, court-ready language. Avoid anthropomorphism. Tie every assertion to evidence.

**Factor 1: Primary Caregiver History & Continuity**
- Distinguish burdensome care (medication, nighttime emergencies, chronic condition management) from recreational interaction (play, social media posts)
- Define primary caregiving operationally: routine daily tasks, medical decision-making, service coordination, emergency fallback
- Show pre-separation caregiving pattern to preempt "wrongful withholding" arguments
- Do not stereotype caregiving roles by gender; account for delegated care (e.g., hired dog walker)

**Factor 2: Financial Capacity & Willingness**
- Compile historical spending by each party with receipts/statements
- Project foreseeable costs based on pet's age and conditions
- Note any lapses in vaccinations, preventive medications, or delayed care
- This is not a wealth test — focus on demonstrated willingness and practical capacity
- Court-ordered pet support authority must be [VERIFY]'d

**Factor 3: Living Environment & Logistics**
- Compare proposed environments on: housing type, yard access, park proximity, vet proximity, pet policy restrictions, other animals/children, caretaker schedule, travel frequency
- Assess housing certainty (executed lease vs. contingent plans)
- Match environment to pet's specific needs (exercise requirements, anxiety, quiet needs)
- For temporary orders: emphasize present stability. For final orders: account for longer-term plans.

**Factor 4: Emotional Bond & Attachment**
- Use observable behavioral indicators only: proximity-seeking, comfort behavior in stress contexts, separation anxiety patterns, training responsiveness, daycare/boarding notes
- Rely on third-party records and long-term patterns over staged demonstrations
- If expert evidence (trainer declaration) is contemplated, flag admissibility requirements [VERIFY for jurisdiction]
- In property-only jurisdictions, calibrate: bond arguments support equitable settlement but may carry less judicial weight

**Factor 5: Safety & Welfare Concerns**
- Document allegations of harm, threats involving the pet, use of pet as tool of coercive control
- If protective orders exist, address exchange logistics (neutral sites, third-party intermediaries)
- Never disclose confidential location information

**Factor 6: Willingness to Facilitate Contact**
- Assess which party is more likely to honor a shared schedule
- Use cautiously — do not import child custody terminology unless the jurisdiction explicitly supports it

For each factor, include an **adversarial awareness** note: identify how opposing counsel will attack and preempt with evidence.

---

## Step 4: Recommendation

Propose one primary recommendation and at least one fallback, each tied to the factor analysis.

**Primary possession template elements:**
- Who retains possession
- Transfer logistics and timeline
- Document updates (microchip, license, vet authorized-agent forms) — specify deadline
- Whether/what structured contact the other party receives
- Conditions on contact (non-interference, compliance with medical regimen)

**Shared schedule template elements** (if parties insist):
- Minimize transitions — avoid high-frequency rotations for anxious animals
- Exchange location (neutral site)
- Holiday/travel rules and right of first refusal for boarding
- Medical decision-making protocol (routine vs. emergency)
- Cost-sharing formula
- End-of-life care protocol
- Dispute resolution mechanism
- Self-executing default provisions if schedule breaks down

**Strategic note:** In high-conflict cases, recommend sole ownership with defined contact over joint ownership with visitation. Joint arrangements invite continued litigation; police typically will not enforce pet visitation orders, and many courts refuse to oversee ongoing pet visitation disputes [VERIFY for jurisdiction].

## Guidelines

- **Jurisdiction first** — never proceed without confirming the forum; analysis is highly jurisdiction-dependent
- **Verify all citations** — use web search or mark [VERIFY]; citation hallucination is the primary risk
- **Evidence-anchored** — every material assertion needs a source ("vet record dated...," "receipt from...," "text message on...")
- **Flag gaps** — where the record is incomplete, highlight and propose how to fill (subpoena, declaration, discovery)
- **Acknowledge weaknesses** — if opposing party's name is on the adoption contract, address it and explain why other factors outweigh
- **Consistency check** — ensure caretaking claims align with employment schedules and lifestyle evidence
- **No child custody importation** — do not mimic child custody language or standards unless the jurisdiction explicitly authorizes it for animals
- **Professional responsibility** — ABA Model Rules 1.1, 1.6, 3.3, 4.1 apply; label contested assertions as "alleged," "disputed," or "reported"
- **Confidentiality** — assume all client-provided facts are confidential; flag sensitive facts before any external sharing
- **Conflict check prompt** — remind attorney-user to confirm no conflicts under Rule 1.7

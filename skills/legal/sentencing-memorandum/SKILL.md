# Sentencing Memorandum (Defense)

Drafts a defense sentencing memorandum advocating for the most favorable sentence, presenting the defendant as a complete person and proposing a just sentence.

## Prerequisites

1. **Presentence report (PSR)** — USSG calculations, criminal history, personal history
2. **Objections to PSR** — factual or legal objections to the probation officer's calculations
3. **Guidelines calculation** — offense level, criminal history category, guidelines range
4. **Defendant's history** — personal background, family, education, employment, health, military service
5. **Letters of support** — from family, employers, community members, clergy
6. **Mitigating evidence** — mental health records, substance abuse treatment, trauma history
7. **Victim impact** — restitution calculations, victim statements
8. **Proposed sentence** — what the defense is requesting and why

## Output Structure

### Introduction

- Acknowledge the offense and its seriousness
- Introduce the defendant as a person, not just a case number
- Preview the requested sentence and why it serves justice

### Guidelines Analysis

**Offense Level Calculation**:
- Base offense level and applicable specific offense characteristics
- Adjustments (role, obstruction, acceptance of responsibility)
- Objections to PSR calculations (with legal authority)
- Correct guidelines range

**Criminal History**:
- Category and score
- Objections (if any — over-representation of criminal history)
- Context for prior offenses

**Departures and Variances**:
- Grounds for departure (USSG §§ 5K1.1, 5K2.0, etc.)
- Grounds for variance under 18 U.S.C. § 3553(a)
- Government motion for downward departure (if cooperating)

### The § 3553(a) Factors

**Nature and circumstances of the offense**:
- Context for the criminal conduct
- Defendant's role (minimal, minor, or distinguish from more culpable actors)
- Aberrant behavior vs. pattern

**History and characteristics of the defendant**:
- Childhood and upbringing (ACEs, trauma, poverty)
- Education and achievements
- Employment history and work ethic
- Family relationships and responsibilities (minor children, dependents, caretaking)
- Military service
- Community involvement and contributions
- Physical and mental health conditions
- Substance abuse history and treatment
- Age and its relevance to recidivism risk

**Need for the sentence to reflect the seriousness of the offense, promote respect for the law, and provide just punishment**

**Need for deterrence** — general and specific

**Need to protect the public** — low recidivism risk evidence

**Need for rehabilitation** — treatment programs, educational opportunities

**Kinds of sentences available** — probation, home confinement, intermittent confinement, community service

**Guidelines range** — advisory nature, parsimony principle

**Need to avoid unwarranted sentencing disparities** — comparable cases with lower sentences

### Mitigating Factors

Present in order of persuasive strength:
- Each factor with supporting evidence (records, declarations, letters)
- Connection to reduced culpability or reduced recidivism risk
- How the factor supports the requested sentence

### Proposed Sentence

- Specific sentence requested (months, conditions)
- Why this sentence is sufficient but not greater than necessary (parsimony principle)
- Proposed conditions of supervised release
- Alternative to incarceration (if appropriate): home confinement, community service, treatment program
- Restitution position

### Restitution

- Agreed amount or basis for dispute
- Ability to pay
- Payment schedule proposal
- Offsets or credits

### Letters of Support

- Summary of letters attached
- Key themes across letters
- Specific commitments (employment, housing, support network)

### Conclusion

- Humanize the defendant one final time
- Restate the requested sentence
- Explain how the sentence serves all § 3553(a) factors

## Guidelines

- The sentencing memo is the most human document in a criminal case — write it that way
- Acknowledge the harm without minimizing — courts respect honesty
- Specific details matter more than generalities ("works 60-hour weeks as a welder to support his three children" > "is a hard worker")
- Address the government's likely arguments for a higher sentence
- Include sentencing statistics for comparable offenses if they support your position
- Support factual claims with exhibits, records, and declarations
- If cooperation, coordinate with the government on the § 5K1.1 motion
- Consider requesting a sentencing hearing to present live testimony from character witnesses

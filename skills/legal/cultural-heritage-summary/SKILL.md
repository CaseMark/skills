# Cultural Heritage Protection Summary

Deliver a concise, defensible update on legal developments affecting cultural heritage protection across jurisdictions and tribunals.

## Prerequisites

1. **Time window** — exact start/end dates for developments covered.
2. **Scope** — jurisdictions, tribunals, and administrative bodies included.
3. **Audience** — government, NGO, cultural institution, enforcement, or mixed.
4. **Citation standard** — Bluebook, OSCOLA, or organization-specific style.
5. **Source access** — decisions, statutes, treaties, official releases, and regulations.

## Output Structure / Process

**Executive Overview (max 1 page)**
- 5–10 bullet headlines of the most material developments.
- One-sentence implications per headline.

**Thematic Sections (use only those supported by the source set)**
- Repatriation and restitution
- Site and monument protection
- Illicit trafficking and enforcement
- Armed conflict and emergency protections
- Underwater cultural heritage
- Intangible cultural heritage and indigenous rights
- Cultural property in trade/customs and export controls
- Digital heritage and documentation

**Development Selection Criteria**
- Direct legal effect (binding decision, statute, regulation, treaty action).
- New interpretation or change in enforcement posture.
- Cross-border or precedent value.
- High-impact items for stakeholders.

**Trend Analysis (final section)**
- 3–6 bullet trends, each with 1–2 supporting examples.
- Include enforcement intensity, restitution momentum, conflict-related protections, and compliance gaps if supported.

**Recommendations (optional, only if requested)**
- 3–7 pragmatic actions tied to specific developments.

## Guidelines

- Verify all dates, holdings, and enactment status; use `[VERIFY]` for uncertain citations or treaty status.
- Do not summarize media reports without primary sources; cite official decisions, statutes, or treaty actions.
- Distinguish binding law from policy guidance or soft law.
- Note treaty applicability only where the jurisdiction has ratified or implemented; otherwise label as persuasive or non-binding.
- Avoid advocacy tone; present neutral legal analysis.
- Use culturally sensitive terminology and accurately identify indigenous or source communities.
- If a development affects return, restitution, or title, state the exact legal basis (statute, treaty, or court rule).
- Flag unresolved issues, splits, or pending appeals explicitly.
- Keep length within the agreed window (default 3–7 pages).

# Urban Planning Law Summary

Produces a structured legal briefing on zoning, land use, and environmental compliance issues affecting an urban development project or municipal planning initiative.

## Prerequisites

1. **Project documents** — zoning ordinances, comprehensive plans, land use applications, site plans
2. **Environmental materials** — EIS/EA reports, wetland delineations, traffic/noise studies, agency correspondence
3. **Regulatory filings** — variance applications, conditional use permits, subdivision plats, overlay district maps
4. **Dispute history** (if any) — public hearing transcripts, opposition letters, related litigation materials

## Output Structure

### 1. Executive Overview

3–4 sentences capturing the most critical legal issues, highest risks, and immediate action items.

### 2. Zoning Law Analysis

| Element | Detail Required |
|---|---|
| Current zoning designation | Classification, overlay districts, special planning zones |
| Proposed use compatibility | By right / conditional / nonconforming status |
| Dimensional compliance | Setbacks, height, FAR, lot coverage, parking/loading |
| Relief needed | Variance, rezoning, special exception — identify type and standard |
| Nonconforming use issues | Grandfathered status, expansion/abandonment rules |

For each conflict identified, state: (a) the applicable regulation, (b) how the project deviates, (c) the available remedy (administrative relief, legislative action, or legal challenge).

### 3. Land Use Disputes & Regulatory Conflicts

Assess each applicable issue:

- [ ] Neighbor/community opposition — standing, grounds, organized or individual
- [ ] Comprehensive plan consistency — required vs. advisory in jurisdiction
- [ ] Vested rights / development agreements
- [ ] Takings or inverse condemnation exposure
- [ ] Federal/state preemption conflicts
- [ ] Procedural compliance — public notice, hearing requirements, appeal deadlines
- [ ] Sequential vs. concurrent approval dependencies

Flag procedural defects specifically — these are the most common grounds for challenge.

### 4. Environmental Compliance

| Category | Key Items |
|---|---|
| NEPA/SEPA status | EIS, EA, or categorical exclusion; adequacy of review |
| Protected resources | Wetlands, endangered species, historic properties, floodplains |
| Required permits | EPA, Army Corps (§404), state environmental agency, local conservation |
| Impact studies | Stormwater, air quality, noise, traffic — status and findings |
| Mitigation measures | Required conditions of approval, monitoring obligations |

### 5. Timeline & Procedural Map

- Critical deadlines (appeals, comment periods, permit expiration)
- Sequential vs. concurrent approval paths
- Estimated timeline risks and bottleneck approvals

### 6. Risk Assessment & Next Steps

For each identified issue, assign risk level:

| Risk Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **High** | Could block or significantly delay project; immediate legal attention required |
| **Medium** | Requires strategic handling; manageable with proper planning |
| **Low** | Routine compliance; monitor but unlikely to impede |

Conclude with:
- Issues requiring immediate legal attention
- Prioritized regulatory approval sequence
- Additional expert consultation needed (environmental, traffic, historic preservation)
- Opportunities for negotiated resolution or administrative relief

## Guidelines

- Maintain neutral, objective tone — do not advocate for development or preservation interests
- Cite specific ordinance sections, statute numbers, and case law with sufficient detail to locate sources; use `[VERIFY]` for uncertain citations
- When documents contain conflicting information or ambiguities, flag explicitly rather than assuming
- Ensure accessibility to non-lawyer readers (municipal officials, developers) while preserving legal precision
- Target 2–5 pages depending on project complexity
- Note jurisdiction-specific variations — zoning enabling authority, Dillon's Rule vs. home rule status, and state environmental review requirements vary significantly

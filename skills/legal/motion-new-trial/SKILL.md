# Motion for New Trial

Drafts a post-verdict motion asking the trial court to set aside a jury verdict and order a new trial, with ground-by-ground factual and legal support drawn from the trial record.

## Prerequisites

1. **Verdict information** — date, charges of conviction, case number, court name
2. **Trial transcripts** — with page/line citations for key testimony, rulings, objections
3. **Exhibit list** — numbered exhibits referenced at trial
4. **Jury instructions** — as given and any refused defense instructions
5. **Applicable rule** — state or federal rule of criminal procedure authorizing the motion (e.g., Fed. R. Crim. P. 33; state equivalent)
6. **Post-trial materials** (if any) — newly discovered evidence, affidavits, expert reports

## Output Structure

### 1. Caption & Header

Standard jurisdiction-format caption consistent with prior filings. Include:
- Court name, case number, defendant's full legal name
- Title: "DEFENDANT'S MOTION FOR NEW TRIAL"

### 2. Introduction (½–1 page)

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Procedural authority | Cite specific rule number and statutory basis |
| Verdict date & charges | Identify conviction counts |
| Relief sought | New trial; evidentiary hearing if applicable |
| Thesis | One-sentence summary of why verdict should be set aside |

### 3. Grounds for New Trial

Draft each ground as a separately labeled section. Include only grounds supported by the record. For each ground, follow this structure:

```
## GROUND [N]: [LABEL]

### Factual Basis
- Specific record citations (witness name, transcript page:line)
- Exhibit references by number
- Quotes from rulings, instructions, or testimony

### Legal Standard
- Governing test from controlling authority
- Key cases granting relief on this ground

### Application
- Map facts to each element of the legal standard
- Show prejudice / effect on verdict

### Anticipate Rebuttal
- Address harmless error, waiver, or procedural default arguments
```

**Standard grounds checklist** (assert only those supported):

| Ground | Key Elements | Typical Standard |
|---|---|---|
| **Weight of evidence** | Verdict is a miscarriage of justice despite jury deference | Evidence so preponderates against verdict that it would be unconscionable to let it stand |
| **Newly discovered evidence** | (1) Discovered post-trial, (2) not discoverable with due diligence pre-trial, (3) material, not cumulative, (4) would probably produce different result | All four prongs required |
| **Prosecutorial misconduct** | Identify specific acts; show they deprived defendant of fair trial | Conduct so egregious it undermined fundamental fairness |
| **Juror misconduct** | Extraneous information, improper communications, undisclosed bias | Reasonable possibility misconduct influenced verdict |
| **Ineffective assistance of counsel** | (1) Performance below professional standards, (2) prejudice — reasonable probability of different result | Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984) |
| **Judicial/legal error** | Erroneous evidentiary rulings, jury instructions, or procedural rulings affecting substantial rights | Abuse of discretion; not harmless beyond reasonable doubt |

### 4. Memorandum of Points and Authorities

- For each ground: legal standard → controlling authority → application to facts
- Cite in Bluebook format; use jurisdiction-specific appellate decisions
- Distinguish unfavorable precedent explicitly
- Structure: topic sentence → authority → record-specific application

### 5. Prayer for Relief

Request:
- Court set aside the verdict on specified counts
- New trial be ordered
- Evidentiary hearing (if grounds involve facts outside the trial record — newly discovered evidence, juror misconduct, IAC)

### 6. Signature Block & Certificate of Service

## Guidelines

- **Record-grounded**: Every factual assertion must cite transcript page:line, exhibit number, or docket entry — never make unsupported factual claims
- **Preserved objections**: Verify each issue was preserved at trial; if not, argue plain error or structural error as appropriate
- **Tone**: Professional and measured, even when alleging misconduct; focus on legal merit, not rhetoric
- **Timeliness**: Note the filing deadline under the applicable rule (Fed. R. Crim. P. 33: 14 days post-verdict for most grounds; 3 years for newly discovered evidence) [VERIFY]
- **Jurisdiction-specific rules**: Confirm local rule requirements for page limits, formatting, and whether a supporting brief or affidavits must be filed separately
- **Do not fabricate citations**: If controlling authority is unknown, flag with [VERIFY] and describe the legal principle
- **Distinguish from appeal**: This motion is directed to the trial court's discretion, not appellate review — frame arguments accordingly

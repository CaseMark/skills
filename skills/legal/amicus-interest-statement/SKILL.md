# Amicus Curiae Interest Statement

Drafts the "Interest of Amicus Curiae" section — the credibility gateway that tells a court why the amicus is a legitimate, useful participant with a unique perspective the parties cannot provide.

## Prerequisites

1. **Case context** — caption, court, docket number, procedural stage, questions presented, decision below
2. **Amicus identity** — full legal name, entity type, founding year, headquarters, membership size/scope, coalition status
3. **Mission & expertise** — public mission statement, core programs, relevant publications, prior amicus filings, domain expertise aligned to the legal issue
4. **Concrete stake** — who is affected by the ruling, through what mechanisms (compliance costs, enforcement changes, chilled conduct, etc.)
5. **Parties' briefs** (if available) — to identify what perspective the amicus uniquely adds
6. **Disclosure facts** — confirm: (a) who authored the brief, (b) whether any party/counsel funded it, (c) any other monetary contributors
7. **Governing rules** — identify which court rules apply (FRAP 29, S.Ct. Rule 37, state rules); if unknown, flag for jurisdictional compliance check

If any prerequisite is missing, request it before producing a final draft.

## Drafting Process

### Step 1: Anchor to the Legal Issue

Open by connecting the amicus directly to the specific legal question presented. Mirror the language of the questions presented.

**Template:**
> Amicus curiae [Organization] submits this brief to assist the Court in resolving whether [legal issue in case-specific terms]. Because [Organization] [work tied to the issue], the Court's resolution will have significant consequences for [identified constituency] and for the practical administration of [system/market/program].

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Name the specific statute, constitutional provision, or rule at issue | Write a generic organizational biography |
| Distinguish amicus perspective from parties' arguments | Recite broad values ("fairness," "justice") untethered to the question |
| Show why *this* amicus in *this* case on *this* question | Draft something recyclable across unrelated cases |

### Step 2: Describe the Organization with Verifiable Specifics

Provide concise, fact-based institutional description. Courts distrust puffery; use concrete indicia of credibility.

**Template:**
> [Organization] is a [entity type] founded in [year] that represents [members/constituency] across [geographic scope]. Its work includes [2-4 core activities], including [activity relevant to the legal issue].

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Use verifiable facts (membership numbers, founding year, programs) | Use unsupported superlatives ("leading," "premier," "renowned") |
| Be candid about advocacy posture | Overclaim neutrality when public posture suggests otherwise |
| Disclose coalition composition when filing jointly | List programs unconnected to the case |

### Step 3: Establish Expertise Mapped to the Doctrinal Choice

Identify 2-3 "expertise pillars" that correspond to the legal issue and connect them to what the brief will address.

- Operational experience implementing the statutory scheme at issue
- Technical knowledge of the regulated subject
- Published research or longitudinal data
- Professional standards or industry norms
- Litigation pattern awareness and remedial design experience

**Template:**
> Amicus has particular expertise relevant to the Court's analysis of [issue], including (1) [pillar], which informs how [legal rule] functions in practice; and (2) [pillar], which bears on [administrability/reliance/consequences] of adopting [proposed standard].

### Step 4: Articulate Concrete Non-Party Impact

Translate doctrine into real-world effects using causal chains, not conjecture.

**Template:**
> If the Court holds that [rule], then [mechanism], which would [practical consequence]. Because [Organization] works directly with [group/system], it has a concrete perspective on how [test/standard] will shape [behavior/administration].

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Define affected populations specifically | Assert "all Americans" when a narrower group is credible |
| Describe mechanisms (compliance costs, enforcement shifts, chilling effects) | Make speculative empirical claims without support |
| Keep claims bounded and plausible | Argue the merits — save that for the argument section |

### Step 5: Calibrate Tone to Court and Role

**Neutral expertise posture:**
> Amicus does not file to support any party as such, but to assist the Court by providing [technical/historical/industry] context relevant to the proper interpretation of [provision].

**Stakeholder advocate posture:**
> Amicus supports [party position] because the decision below threatens to [consequence] for [constituency], whom amicus serves through [programs/representation].

Draft as though every sentence will be excerpted by opposing counsel.

### Step 6: Harmonize with Required Disclosures

Do NOT include disclosure language unless the user affirmatively confirms accuracy. When confirmed:

> Pursuant to [Rule], amicus curiae states that no counsel for a party authored this brief in whole or in part, and no person or entity other than amicus curiae or its counsel made a monetary contribution intended to fund the preparation or submission of this brief.

Mark as `[VERIFY — confirm governing rule and underlying facts]` if any element is unconfirmed.

## Key Rules Reference

| Forum | Rule | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| U.S. Supreme Court | Rule 37.1 | Brief must bring relevant matter not already presented by parties |
| U.S. Supreme Court | Rule 37.6 | Authorship and funding disclosures required |
| Federal Circuits | FRAP 29(a)(4)(D) | Statement of amicus's interest required |
| Federal Circuits | FRAP 29(a)(4)(E) | Authorship and funding disclosures required |
| California | Rule of Court 8.520(f) | Must state relationship to parties beyond funding; identify other filings on same issue |
| 7th Circuit | Local practice | Heightened skepticism of "me-too" briefs — must demonstrate unique contribution |
| State courts | Varies | Check local rules for motion-for-leave requirements and extra-record fact restrictions |

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] **Relevance test**: Can a clerk explain in one sentence why this amicus belongs in this case?
- [ ] **Issue tethering**: Does the statement name the specific legal question or standard at stake?
- [ ] **Non-redundancy**: Does it articulate what the amicus uniquely provides beyond the parties?
- [ ] **Mission alignment**: Is the stated interest logically consistent with the organization's mission?
- [ ] **Factual support**: Every claim (membership, years, scope, programs) traceable to public or internal records?
- [ ] **Disclosure consistency**: No contradictions between Interest Statement and separate disclosure section?
- [ ] **Adversarial resilience**: No sentence that, excerpted, makes the amicus appear biased, unqualified, or improperly involved?
- [ ] **Procedural compliance**: Required disclosures present, placed correctly, and factually accurate?

## Guidelines

- Flag all organizational facts for attorney verification before filing
- Mark any unverified legal citation with `[VERIFY]`
- Do not resolve conflicts of interest — flag indicators for attorney review
- Do not draft disclosure language without affirmative user confirmation of underlying facts
- When the governing court is unidentified, draft substantively and mark all rule-dependent statements for jurisdictional compliance review
- Keep the Interest Statement to 1-3 paragraphs; this is not the argument section
- All output requires licensed attorney review before filing

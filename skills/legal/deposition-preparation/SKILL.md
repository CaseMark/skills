# Deposition Preparation

Produces a deposition preparation package: a strategic examination plan tied to legal elements, time allocation, exhibit integration, impeachment materials, and witness-specific adaptations.

## Deliverables

Three output modes:
1. **Full Prep Package** — Complete strategic plan, outline, exhibits, impeachment tables
2. **At-the-Table Outline** — Condensed examination outline for use during the deposition
3. **Defending Pack** — Witness preparation agenda, anticipated topics, objection reference

## Prerequisites

Before drafting, gather:
- Case theory and key claims/defenses with elements
- Witness identity, role, and relationship to the case
- Posture: taking or defending
- Forum: federal (FRCP 30) vs. state (identify jurisdiction)
- Time limits and any court orders (scheduling, protective, ESI protocol)
- Available documents: pleadings, written discovery responses, prior testimony, key exhibits

If any prerequisite is unavailable, note the assumption and proceed. Flag assumptions for attorney confirmation.

## Process

### Step 1: Build the Issue Map

Create a table mapping each examination topic to the legal element or defense it serves:

| Topic | Legal Element / Defense | Key Documents | Priority |
|-------|----------------------|---------------|----------|
| [Topic] | [Element it proves/undermines] | [Exhibit refs] | High/Med/Low |

Every topic must tie to an element or defense. Delete topics that don't serve the case theory.

### Step 2: Profile the Witness

Determine witness type and adapt approach:
- **Party witness**: Lock into positions, secure admissions, explore knowledge boundaries
- **Fact witness**: Establish timeline, authenticate documents, identify other witnesses
- **Expert witness**: Probe methodology, test assumptions, explore scope of opinions
- **30(b)(6) designee**: Confirm topics of designation, bind the organization, explore preparation
- **Apex witness**: Establish actual knowledge, avoid overly broad topics per apex doctrine

### Step 3: Draft the Examination Outline

Structure the outline as a record-building sequence, not a topic list:
- Open with background/foundation questions (low stakes, build rapport or establish baseline)
- Progress through each issue map topic in strategic order
- For each topic: foundation → key questions → document confrontation → pin-down/admission
- Close with catch-all and reservation of rights

Use the **commit-credit-confront** method for impeachment sequences:
1. **Commit**: Get the witness to commit to a position
2. **Credit**: Establish the credibility of the impeaching source
3. **Confront**: Present the contradiction

### Step 4: Integrate Exhibits

Create an exhibit integration table:

| Exhibit | Description | Outline Section | Purpose | Authentication Witness |
|---------|-------------|----------------|---------|----------------------|
| Ex. A | [Doc name] | Topic 3 | Impeachment | Deponent |

### Step 5: Adapt for Posture

**Taking the deposition**: Prioritize discovery (open questions) vs. preservation (closed questions leading to admissions) based on case stage.

**Defending the deposition**: Prepare witness on anticipated topics. Note: do not coach testimony — confine preparation to familiarizing the witness with the process and reviewing relevant documents (FRE 612 considerations).

### Step 6: Time Allocation

Allocate the available time (default: 7 hours FRCP 30(d)(1)) across topics proportional to priority:

| Topic | Allocated Time | Priority |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| [Topic] | [Minutes] | High/Med/Low |
| Buffer/follow-up | 15-30 min | — |

## Compliance

- **FRCP 30**: 7-hour default, one day. Leave must be sought for exceeding limits.
- **FRCP 26(b)(1)**: Discovery must be proportional to the needs of the case.
- **FRE 612**: Documents used to refresh recollection may be discoverable.
- **FRE 613**: Prior inconsistent statements — lay foundation before impeachment at trial.
- **Professional responsibility**: Do not coach testimony, maintain candor, comply with protective orders.

All output requires attorney review. This is not legal advice.

## Jurisdiction Adaptations

For state courts, verify:
- Time limits (many states differ from the 7-hour federal default)
- Objection rules (some states require "form only" objections; others allow speaking objections)
- Apex witness protections (varies by jurisdiction)
- Corporate designee rules (state equivalents of FRCP 30(b)(6))

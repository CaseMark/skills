# Apex Witness Deposition

Navigates apex doctrine strategy and drafts supporting documents for seeking or defending depositions of high-ranking executives and officials.

## Prerequisites

1. **Witness identification** — title, organizational role, position in hierarchy
2. **Party position** — seeking the deposition (plaintiff) or defending it (defendant)
3. **Discovery record** — depositions taken, documents produced, interrogatories served and answered
4. **Jurisdiction** — federal or state court; circuit/district if known
5. **Evidence of involvement** — documents, depo testimony, or communications tying the executive to key facts

## Apex Doctrine Framework

The apex doctrine protects senior officials from deposition unless the moving party satisfies all three elements:

| Element | What to Demonstrate |
|---|---|
| **Unique personal knowledge** | Executive personally made decisions, signed documents, or communicated directly on the key issue |
| **Not available elsewhere** | Lower-level witnesses and documents don't fill the gap; other discovery has been taken and failed |
| **Not harassment/burden** | Deposition is narrowly targeted, not designed to disrupt the executive or the organization |

**Apex witnesses include:** CEO, President, COO, CFO, General Counsel, Board Chair, agency heads, senior elected officials.

**Jurisdictional note:** Federal courts apply apex protection through FRCP 26(c) proportionality; some states have explicit apex doctrine rules. Protection is not absolute. [VERIFY local circuit authority before filing.]

## Part A: Seeking an Apex Deposition

### A1 — Threshold Checklist

Complete all items before proceeding — failure on any weakens the motion:

- [ ] Executive personally involved in the decisions or events at issue
- [ ] Executive made, approved, or directed the key acts
- [ ] Executive communicated directly with relevant parties
- [ ] Lower-level depositions taken — witnesses confirmed they lack the needed knowledge
- [ ] Document discovery complete — gaps remain that only the executive can fill
- [ ] Interrogatories served asking opposing party to identify who has personal knowledge of key topics

### A2 — Building the Record

1. **Depose lower-level witnesses first** — lock in testimony that executive was the decision-maker
2. **Pull all executive-level communications** — emails, signed documents, meeting attendance lists
3. **Serve targeted interrogatories:**
   - "Identify all persons with knowledge of [topic]."
   - "State whether [Executive] has personal knowledge of [specific facts]."
   - "Describe [Executive's] involvement in [key decision]."
4. **Document the gap** — chart what you know, what's missing, and why only the executive can provide it

### A3 — Motion to Compel: Core Arguments

1. **Personal involvement** — cite depo testimony, documents, signatures, meeting records showing direct participation
2. **Unique knowledge** — demonstrate no other witness can testify to the executive's state of mind, reasoning, or directives
3. **Exhaustion** — enumerate depositions taken, interrogatory answers received, and document review completed
4. **Proposed limitations** — offer 2–3 hour cap, restricted topic list, remote option; courts are more receptive with guardrails

### A4 — Examination Outline (if deposition permitted)

Anchor every topic in the executive's personal knowledge; skip operational detail they won't have.

| Topic Area | Sample Questions |
|---|---|
| Decision-making | "You personally made this decision?" / "What was your reasoning?" |
| Information at the time | "What did you know when you made that call?" / "Who briefed you?" |
| Alternatives considered | "What options did you weigh?" / "Why did you choose this path?" |
| Directives issued | "What did you instruct [subordinate] to do?" / "Did you follow up?" |

**Execution rules:** Lead with documents the executive signed or sent. If the executive doesn't know, move on — don't linger. Stay professional; executives are experienced and games backfire.

## Part B: Defending an Apex Deposition

### B1 — Objection, Meet-and-Confer, and Protective Order

**Step 1 — Respond to deposition notice:**

> We object to the deposition of [CEO] under the apex doctrine. [CEO] is the chief executive of [Company] and has no personal knowledge of the operational matters at issue, which were handled by [department/individuals]. Plaintiff has not yet deposed [alternative witnesses] or sought 30(b)(6) testimony on these topics.
>
> We offer: (1) deposition of [Alternative Witness] who was directly involved; (2) 30(b)(6) designation on the noticed topics; (3) written interrogatories to [CEO] on any specific topic where personal involvement is asserted. Please contact us to discuss. If we cannot reach agreement, we will seek a protective order.

**Step 2 — Meet and confer:** Identify exactly what information plaintiff seeks; propose 30(b)(6) or subordinate alternatives; confirm whether written discovery would satisfy the need.

**Step 3 — Motion for Protective Order (FRCP 26(c)) arguments:**

1. Executive lacks personal knowledge — not involved in the operational events at issue
2. Alternative sources exist — lower-level witnesses and 30(b)(6) cover all identified topics
3. Plaintiff has not exhausted alternatives before seeking apex deposition
4. Undue burden — executive's organizational responsibilities weigh against deposition on matters handled by subordinates
5. Alternative relief: if deposition is permitted, request cap of [X] hours limited to [specific topics]

### B2 — Preparing the Executive Witness

| Challenge | Preparation Approach |
|---|---|
| Overconfidence | Run a mock cross-examination with skilled questioning to calibrate |
| Public spokesperson instincts | "This is not investor relations — answer only what's asked, nothing more" |
| Used to controlling the room | "You don't control the pace, topics, or narrative here" |
| Limited prep availability | Prioritize every document they signed or sent; drill hard on scope boundaries |
| Tendency to fill silence | Practice stopping after the answer; silence is not your problem |

**Key coaching points:**
- Testify only to personal, firsthand knowledge — not what staff reported to you
- "I wasn't personally involved" and "that was handled by [name/department]" are complete, valid answers
- Do not speculate about subordinates' actions, intent, or reasoning
- Listen to the full question before answering; counsel may object
- Never volunteer information beyond the question asked

### B3 — During the Deposition

- Object when questions exceed the executive's personal knowledge: *"Objection — calls for speculation about matters handled by subordinates."*
- Object when questions belong to the 30(b)(6) designee: *"Objection — this question is more appropriately directed to the corporate representative."*
- Invoke court-ordered time or topic limits explicitly when reached
- Request a break if a harassment pattern develops; document it for the record

## Key Citations

- *Salter v. Upjohn Co.*, 593 F.2d 649 (5th Cir. 1979) [VERIFY] — early apex doctrine foundation
- *Thomas v. IBM*, 48 F.3d 478 (10th Cir. 1995) [VERIFY] — apex protection requirements
- *Apple Inc. v. Samsung Elecs.*, 282 F.R.D. 259 (N.D. Cal. 2012) [VERIFY] — high-profile apex dispute
- FRCP 26(c) — protective order authority; proportionality framework for apex disputes

## Guidelines

- Exhaust lower-level depositions before seeking an apex deposition — courts require it and will ask
- Propose limitations proactively in any motion to compel; courts are far more receptive
- Do not file a motion to compel without documentary evidence of personal executive involvement
- Apex protection applies to senior government officials under a parallel doctrine [VERIFY: Harlow v. Fitzgerald framework]
- Jurisdiction controls — verify whether your circuit or state applies a bright-line apex rule or general proportionality before briefing

# Legal Memorandum

Drafts an objective, internally focused legal memorandum that analyzes specific legal questions and provides reasoned conclusions with strategic recommendations.

## Prerequisites

1. **Legal question(s)** — specific issues requiring analysis
2. **Relevant facts** — documents, deposition excerpts, evidence, or client-provided narrative
3. **Jurisdiction** — controlling law (state/federal)
4. **Audience** — senior attorney (sophisticated analysis) or client (practical emphasis)
5. **Applicable authority** — statutes, regulations, key case law if known

## Output Structure

### 1. Header

```
MEMORANDUM

DATE:    [Date]
TO:      [Recipient name and title]
FROM:    [Author name and title]
RE:      [Specific subject — e.g., "Likelihood of Success on Summary Judgment — Causation in Doe v. Metro Transit"]
```

- Subject line must identify the legal issue, not just the case name
- Include matter/case reference number if available

### 2. Questions Presented

Draft 1–3 questions using the **Under-Does-When** format:

| Component | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **Under** | Relevant law, statute, or rule |
| **Does/Is/Can** | Legal conclusion sought |
| **When** | Key facts driving the analysis |

- Frame so they can be answered yes/no or with a short definitive response
- Prioritize by importance to client objectives
- No conclusory or leading language

### 3. Brief Answer

For each question:

- Open with **Yes**, **No**, or **Probably [yes/no]**
- Follow with 2–3 sentences of critical reasoning
- Reference the controlling rule and dispositive facts

### 4. Statement of Facts

| Guideline | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Organization** | Chronological (transaction/procedural issues) or topical (multiple legal theories) |
| **Scope** | Only legally relevant facts + necessary background context |
| **Tone** | Objective — no editorial commentary |
| **Sourcing** | Cite specific documents, depositions, exhibits parenthetically |
| **Disputed facts** | Flag explicitly when facts are contested or unclear |

Include a disclaimer that the analysis is based on facts as presented and may change if additional facts emerge.

### 5. Analysis / Discussion

Use **CREAC** for each issue:

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| **C — Conclusion** | State conclusion for this issue upfront |
| **R — Rule** | Articulate the legal rule; cite primary authority (statutes, case law); quote key language where precision matters |
| **E — Explanation** | Show how courts have applied the rule; use factually analogous cases; explain reasoning, not just holdings |
| **A — Application** | Apply rule to client's facts; draw direct parallels to precedent; address counterarguments proactively; acknowledge weaknesses |
| **C — Conclusion** | Restate conclusion for the issue |

Checklist:
- [ ] Use controlling jurisdiction's law
- [ ] Cite primary authority over secondary sources
- [ ] Distinguish unfavorable cases rather than ignoring them
- [ ] Break multi-part tests into sub-analyses with subheaders
- [ ] Cross-reference related issues

### 6. Conclusion

- Directly answer each question presented
- Summarize key reasoning without introducing new arguments
- Provide **strategic recommendations**: actions, risks, alternatives
- Identify **next steps**: deadlines, further research, litigation/negotiation strategy, information gaps

## Guidelines

- **Objectivity**: Present both sides honestly; do not hide unfavorable facts or authority
- **Audience calibration**: For attorneys, emphasize doctrinal nuance and strategic implications; for clients, emphasize practical consequences and business impact
- **Citation accuracy**: Cite all legal authority properly; mark any unverified citation with `[VERIFY]`
- **Concision**: Lead with conclusions; avoid burying analysis in lengthy exposition
- **Counterarguments**: Always address the strongest opposing position
- **Recommendations**: Must be practical and actionable, not purely academic
- **Scope disclaimer**: Note that analysis is limited to facts and authority reviewed; flag areas where additional research may be warranted

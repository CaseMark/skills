# Building Value Creation Plans

Structures a quantified, initiative-level value creation plan (VCP) spanning the 100-day sprint through full hold period, decomposing target returns into revenue growth, margin expansion, multiple arbitrage, and leverage paydown.

## Why This Skill Exists

In private equity, the VCP is the single document that translates an investment thesis into an executable operating playbook. LPs increasingly demand return attribution — they want to see what percentage of gross IRR came from organic EBITDA growth versus margin expansion versus multiple arbitrage versus financial leverage. McKinsey's value creation framework and similar decompositions (Bain's repeatable-model analysis, BCG's value creation methodology) have become table stakes in IC memos and annual LP letters. A VCP that lacks specificity — missing initiative-level P&L impact, unclear ownership, or no phasing — signals an undisciplined hold strategy and erodes GP credibility.

Operating partners are accountable for converting the deal team's underwriting case into realized value. The 100-day plan sets the cadence: management equity alignment (option pool strike prices, vesting gates tied to EBITDA milestones), board reporting rhythms, and early-win identification. A rigorous VCP also protects against downside — when a portfolio company misses plan, the VCP's initiative waterfall shows exactly which lever underperformed, enabling rapid reallocation of operating resources. This skill ensures the agent produces a VCP that a deal partner could present at IC or an operating partner could hand to a management team on Day 1.

## Checkpoint A: Pre-Draft Intake (Mandatory)

Before building the VCP, gather the following from the user:

1. **Deal type**: Platform acquisition, add-on/bolt-on, growth equity minority, or carve-out?
2. **Sector and sub-sector**: e.g., healthcare services / multi-site dental, industrial / specialty chemicals distribution
3. **Target hold period**: Typical 3-5 years — confirm exact underwriting assumption
4. **Entry financials**: LTM Revenue, LTM EBITDA, EBITDA margin, and any known normalizing adjustments
5. **Capital structure at close**: Total debt / EBITDA, equity check size, any seller rollover or earnout
6. **Management equity structure**: Option pool size (% fully diluted), vesting schedule, performance hurdles (MOIC gates, EBITDA thresholds)
7. **Key value levers identified in DD**: Which of revenue growth, margin improvement, M&A / buy-and-build, working capital optimization, or multiple expansion are primary in the thesis?
8. **Known constraints or risks**: Regulatory dependencies, customer concentration, key-person risk, pending litigation

### Documents to Request

- Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) or management presentation
- Quality of Earnings (QofE) report — especially EBITDA adjustments and run-rate bridge
- Commercial due diligence report (market sizing, competitive positioning)
- Operational due diligence findings (technology, supply chain, workforce)
- Historical financials (3 years minimum) with segment-level detail
- Industry benchmarks (e.g., IBIS, Bain sector reports, S&P Capital IQ comps)
- Existing management budget/forecast if available
- Org chart with FTE count by function

## Step 1: Baseline Assessment and EBITDA Bridge

Establish the true starting point by building a normalized EBITDA bridge from reported financials to the underwritten entry case.

## Step 3: Phasing and Ownership — The 100-Day Plan Through Exit

### 3.1 The 100-Day Plan (Day 1 – Day 100)

Structure early wins and foundational work:

| Phase | Days | Priority Initiatives | Owner | Deliverable |
|-------|------|---------------------|-------|-------------|
| Stabilization | 1-30 | Management alignment meetings, cash management controls, quick procurement wins (C1, C5) | Deal lead + Ops partner | Signed management equity docs; 13-week cash flow model |

| Acceleration | 61-100 | Launch top 3 initiatives by EBITDA impact; hire/backfill critical roles; begin M&A pipeline (if applicable) | Initiative owners | Board deck with 100-day results and Year 1 roadmap |

## Step 4: Produce the Deliverable

The final VCP document must contain the following sections in order:

1. **Executive Summary** (1 page): Deal overview, entry valuation, target return, and top 5 initiatives by impact
2. **Baseline Assessment**: Normalized EBITDA bridge, margin benchmarking, return attribution (Step 1 outputs)
3. **Initiative Detail**: Full initiative tables with quantified P&L impact (Step 2 outputs)
4. **Implementation Roadmap**: 100-day plan, Year 1-3 phasing, EBITDA bridge (Step 3 outputs)
5. **KPI Dashboard**: 8-12 KPIs with baseline, target, and measurement frequency
6. **Risk Register**: Top 5-10 risks to plan achievement with mitigation actions
7. **Governance**: Board reporting cadence, initiative review rhythm, escalation triggers
8. **Appendix**: Detailed assumptions, sensitivity tables, benchmarking data sources

Format as a structured document suitable for IC presentation or operating partner handoff. All dollar figures should use consistent units (millions unless otherwise noted). Include page numbers and a table of contents for documents exceeding 10 pages.

## Checkpoint B: Post-Draft Alignment (Mandatory)

Before finalizing, confirm the following with the user:

1. Does the return attribution decomposition match the IC memo's underwriting case?
2. Are initiative-level EBITDA impacts consistent with the QofE-adjusted baseline (no double-counting of adjustments)?
3. Has management reviewed and agreed to initiative ownership assignments and milestone gates?
4. Are inorganic assumptions (acquisition multiples, synergy timing) consistent with the fund's pipeline and sector experience?
5. Does the 100-day plan reflect any known Day 1 constraints (regulatory approvals, closing conditions, transition services agreements)?

## Guidelines

1. **Never fabricate financial figures.** Use placeholder notation ($Xm, X%) and `[VERIFY]` tags for any value not confirmed by source documents. A wrong number in a VCP is worse than a blank.
2. **Distinguish confidence tiers explicitly.** High-confidence initiatives (procurement, headcount) should be separated from speculative ones (new market entry, product launches) in both tables and the EBITDA bridge.
3. **Ground every margin benchmark.** Cite the specific source — Capital IQ comps set, IBIS industry report, or QofE provider benchmarks. "Industry average" without attribution is unacceptable.
4. **Phase initiatives realistically.** Cost savings typically realize 60-80% in Year 1; revenue initiatives ramp over 12-18 months. Do not front-load revenue growth without justification.
5. **Tie the VCP to management incentives.** Reference the management equity structure — if option vesting is gated at 2.5x MOIC, the VCP should show the path to that threshold.
6. **Account for implementation costs.** Every initiative should net out one-time implementation costs (consultant fees, severance, technology spend) against gross savings.
7. **Separate organic from inorganic growth.** LPs and IC members scrutinize blended figures. Always present organic EBITDA growth and M&A contribution as distinct line items in the bridge.
8. **Escalate when data is insufficient.** If the QofE is unavailable or the DD is incomplete, flag the specific gaps rather than building on assumptions. A VCP built on unverified assumptions is a liability, not a plan.
